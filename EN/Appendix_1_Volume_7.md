### 7.1 Opening Statement

This volume is positioned as the **habitation-space and social-foundation layer** of the lunar industrial system. It focuses on answering the following core questions:

> As a lunar base grows from an outpost of a few dozen people into an industrial town of hundreds, and ultimately into an off-world city of thousands or even tens of thousands, where do people live? How are oxygen, water, and food recycled within a closed environment? How do we manage microbial communities, the invisible "hidden crew"? Whether in a deep lava tube or on the exposed lunar surface, how do we create a living environment that is safe, comfortable, and dignified? Under low gravity, how do we enable ordinary people, not only highly trained astronauts, to walk, sleep, and carry out daily life in ways that feel close to Earth? When unavoidable bodily functions encounter the constraints of low-gravity fluid dynamics, how do we design a splash-free, self-cleaning sanitation system? Should dome-farm lighting rely on LEDs or direct natural-light conduits, and when the full co-generation system is accounted for, which option is more efficient? What engineering and human-centered thresholds lie between simply "being able to survive" and truly "living well"?

This volume builds on the following:

- **Volume II (Site Selection and Infrastructure)**: It has already defined radiation-shielding standards, sealing and pressurization schemes for habitation zones, and functional zoning for underground lava tubes. **If the final site is a fully surface-based option, then all habitation spaces must be designed to surface protection standards.**
- **Volume III (Energy Systems)**: It has already provided the low-temperature waste-heat interface required for space heating (30-50°C capillary network), as well as the natural-light conduit lighting concept.
- **Volume V (Manufacturing Systems)**: It has already provided production capacity and quality standards for building materials such as sintered brick and concrete.
- **Volume VI (Transportation Network)**: It has already provided transportation capacity for personnel commuting and logistics distribution.

The core tasks of this volume are:

1. Design a complete expansion roadmap for human habitation, from short-stay outpost to city-scale settlement, **covering both underground and fully surface-based site options**.
2. Plan the detailed equipment parameters and closed-loop mass-flow pathways of the CELSS.
3. Design architectural parameters for residential units, public spaces, and farms under different site conditions.
4. Use an equivalent integrated-system model to rigorously assess dome-farm lighting options, comparing the combined efficiency of LEDs and natural-light conduits under a co-generation framework.
5. Systematically design daily living facilities for all people under low gravity. Trained astronauts must be provided with exercise facilities to counter physiological degradation, while ordinary residents such as travelers and immigrants must be provided with **Earth-like adaptation** facilities so they can walk, sleep, and use sanitation systems in ways that feel close to normal terrestrial life.
6. Establish staged medical-support and food-supply systems for lunar society.
7. Build education and cultural facilities during the later lunar-city phase.
8. Define staged validation criteria for the habitation system.

#### 7.3.2 Closed Water-Oxygen-Carbon Loop

The closed cycling of water, oxygen, and carbon is the core of the life-support system. The system follows the principle of "consume -> recover -> regenerate -> reuse," minimizing dependence on external resupply.

**(a) Water cycle**

- Condensate water (cabin-atmosphere dehumidification): -> filtration (5 um + activated carbon) + UV disinfection -> directly reused as drinking water.
- Sanitation wastewater (showers, laundry): -> membrane bioreactor (MBR, pore size 0.04 um) + reverse osmosis (RO, desalination rate >= 99%) -> reused as sanitation water and irrigation water.
- Urine: -> distillation (low-temperature vacuum distillation, 40-50°C, for reduced energy consumption) + catalytic oxidation (TiO2/UV) -> reused as drinking water (recovery rate >= 98%).
- Plant transpiration water: -> condensate recovery (agricultural-zone dehumidification, finned-tube heat exchanger) -> reused as drinking water.

**(b) Oxygen cycle**

- O2 supply:
  - **Physicochemical pathway (primary)**: Oxygen generation by water electrolysis (solid polymer electrolyzer, efficiency >= 70%). The O2 is purified (catalytic hydrogen removal + drying) and injected into the habitat atmosphere.
  - **Biological pathway (supplementary)**: Oxygen release by plant photosynthesis.
- CO₂ removal:
  - **Physicochemical pathway (primary)**: Molecular-sieve adsorption (13X zeolite, CO₂/N₂ selectivity >= 10) + Sabatier reaction ($CO_2 + 4H_2 \rightarrow CH_4 + 2H_2O$, Ni-based catalyst, 300-400°C, pressure 1-3 MPa). The generated H₂O returns to the water loop; CH₄ is used as FCCVD carbon feedstock or stored as reserve.
  - **Biological pathway (supplementary)**: CO₂ uptake by plant photosynthesis.

**(c) Carbon cycle**

- CO₂ originates from crew respiration and industrial emissions, such as by-product CO₂ from the metal-smelting workshops described in Volume V.
- CO₂ is converted through the Sabatier reaction into CH₄ and H₂O. The H₂O is reused, while the CH₄ enters the carbon-feedstock reserve for CNT synthesis via FCCVD or for use as propellant in lunar landers.
- Plant biomass (inedible portions) undergoes anaerobic digestion or pyrolysis to produce CH₄ and CO₂, returning both to the carbon cycle.

**(d) Microbial control and biosafety**

Within a closed CELSS, microorganisms are not incidental contaminants but indispensable "hidden crew" within the material cycle. Beneficial microbes, such as nitrifying bacteria and fermentation communities, must be actively maintained, while pathogenic and spoilage organisms must be tightly controlled. Microbial management therefore follows a zoned strategy:

- **Beneficial-microbe zones (actively maintained)**: The activated-sludge community in the MBR, the nitrifying bacteria in the aquaponics system, and the methanogenic population in anaerobic digesters form the core of water treatment and solid-waste conversion. Their health is monitored in real time by online sensors for pH, dissolved oxygen, and oxidation-reduction potential. During the outpost phase, the initial strains are pre-deployed from Earth in freeze-dried form. By the industrial-town phase, the system is expected to become self-sustaining through a local strain bank.
- **Residential zones (strictly controlled)**: Physical antimicrobial control is primary, supplemented by passive biological monitoring. All interior wall materials, including SPUA seal layers, aluminum-alloy panels, and UHMWPE textiles, must pass ISO 846:2019, Plastics - Evaluation of the Action of Microorganisms [2], and must not contain nutrient sources for microbial growth. High-touch surfaces such as door handles, control panels, and handrails use silver-ion antimicrobial coatings. The habitat atmosphere passes through HEPA filtration (>= 99.97% efficiency at 0.3 um) to control aerosolized microbial concentration. Routine chemical disinfection is avoided in order to preserve the healthy balance of the human symbiotic microbiome.
- **Medical zone (highest control)**: Isolation rooms operate at negative pressure (-15 Pa). Exhaust air passes through dual HEPA + ultraviolet sterilization (UVC, 254 nm, irradiation dose >= 40 mJ/cm²) before entering the shared ventilation system.
- **Dome farms (ecological control)**: These use a "beneficial microbe occupancy" strategy. Dominant probiotic strains such as *Bacillus subtilis* and *Trichoderma* are periodically inoculated into nutrient solution so they form biofilms around roots and on leaf surfaces, competitively suppressing pathogens. Chemical fungicides and bactericides are not used, in order to protect aquatic organisms within the aquaponics loop.
- **Passive monitoring network**: Distributed microbial sampling points are installed along ventilation ducts, water-treatment outlets, soil substrates, and medical zones (>= 1 point per 200 m²). At critical nodes, such as medical exhaust and drinking-water outlets, rapid daily 16S rRNA amplicon screening (< 2 h turnaround) is performed. Base-wide metagenomic sequencing is performed weekly to establish a microbial baseline map. Any abundance deviation exceeding 3 standard deviations triggers automatic alarm, contamination-source tracing, and intensified base-wide sequencing (once per day until baseline recovery).

### 7.4 Habitat Space Design

#### 7.4.1 Standard Residential Unit

Regardless of whether the site is underground or surface-based, the basic functional requirements of residential units are the same. Every unit must include independent temperature and humidity control, independent ventilation (>= 5 air changes per hour), dimmable LED lighting, two emergency oxygen masks, a communication terminal, and a charging dock for a personal radiation dosimeter.

#### 7.4.2 Underground Lava-Tube Residential Units

Within underground lava tubes, habitation zones are divided into four functional levels, linked either by walking corridors or by electric rail within the tube.

**(a) Individual residential units (living quarters)**

- **Type**: Single-occupancy cabin or double-occupancy family cabin.
- **Dimensions**: About 3.0 m internal diameter x 5.0 m length for single occupancy, or about 4.0 m internal diameter x 7.0 m length for double occupancy; volume about 35-88 m³.
- **Structure**: Aluminum-alloy shell + external SPUA + CFRP + MLI composite seal layer (see Volume II, Section 2.4.2), with inner PE radiation-moderation liner (>= 5 mm) and decorative panels.
- **Configuration**: Independent temperature and humidity control, dimmable LED lighting + end diffusers for natural-light conduits, two emergency oxygen masks, communication terminal.
- **Radiation protection**: Uses >= 30 m of natural basalt overhead to shield GCR and SPE. After PE moderation, annual radiation dose is <= 20 mSv, with a target of <= 10 mSv. Industrial zones remain unpressurized or only low-pressure sealed, focused on dust exclusion, and use the cave's stable temperature for passive heat rejection.

**(b) Shared living zone (one per 20-40 residents)**

- **Functions**: Shared kitchen, dining area, laundry room, and small library / recreation room.
- **Area**: About 100-200 m².
- **Configuration**: End-point water interfaces for the closed-loop water system (kitchen sinks, washing machines); food storage units with temperature zones of -20°C frozen, 4°C refrigerated, and 20°C room-temperature; microwave or induction heating for cooking, since open-flame cooking is not feasible in the lunar vacuum environment.

**(c) Community center (one per 200-500 residents)**

- **Functions**: Gymnasium for low-gravity adaptation training, multi-purpose hall for meetings, performances, and religious observances, small cinema / VR immersion room, medical station (Section 7.7.1), education center (Section 7.8.1).
- **Area**: About 1,000-2,000 m².
- **Natural-light access**: Community centers are located within a 500 m radius below a skylight. A branch diffuser from the main light conduit feeds the central hall, providing illuminance of 200-500 lux to simulate overcast daylight on Earth.

**(d) Dome farm (one per 200-500 residents)**

- **Functions**: Vegetable and fruit cultivation, fish farming (tilapia or carp, chosen for low-oxygen tolerance, fast growth, and resilience to water-quality fluctuations), and leisure walking.
- **Area**: About 500-1,000 m² planting area + 200 m² for pathways and rest areas.
- **Lighting**: Mixed lighting from natural-light conduits providing "cold light" (visible light without infrared heat load) and LED grow lamps (red 660 nm + blue 450 nm), with controllable photoperiods, typically 16 h light / 8 h dark. **Dedicated infrared and ultraviolet LED lamps** provide specific wavelengths for morphogenesis control, secondary-metabolite accumulation, and pest suppression.
- **Crop selection**: Priority is given to short-cycle, high-yield, nutrient-dense crops, including lettuce (about 30 days to harvest), tomato (about 60-80 days), potato (about 90-120 days), strawberry (about 60 days), wheat (about 120 days), and soybean (about 100 days; a key protein crop, with conservative annual hydroponic yield of about 2-4 kg/m² under lunar conditions). Crop rotation ensures fresh vegetables are harvested every day.
- **Fish farming**: Coupled to plant cultivation as an aquaponics loop. Fish waste is converted by nitrifying bacteria into plant nutrients, and water purified by the plants returns to the fish tanks, keeping overall water consumption very low.
- **CO₂ and waste-heat utilization**: CO₂ supplied to dome farms comes from habitat air purification and industrial emissions, with a net CO₂ flow of about 5-20 L/min (standard conditions) per 1,000 m², improving photosynthetic efficiency by more than 30%. Warm-water irrigation is supplied by the low-temperature waste heat described in Volume III, and farm temperature is maintained at 22°C-28°C.
- **Area estimate**: Assuming about 0.5 kg/day of vegetables and fruit per person, 500 people require about 250 kg/day. Using average yields of lettuce (about 20 kg/m²/year) and tomato (about 10 kg/m²/year), about 500-800 m² of planting area is sufficient for the baseline fresh-produce supply. If protein self-sufficiency is also pursued through soybeans and fish, total cultivation area must expand to about 2,000 m² per 500 people, a scale achievable during the industrial-town phase.

#### 7.4.3 Fully Surface-Based Habitat Option: Bermed Dome Clusters

If the final site is a fully surface-based option, such as Shackleton Rim or another lunar surface region, all habitation space adopts **modular bermed domes**. These domes are built from lunar-regolith brick plus CFRP framing, with internal diameter 10-20 m and clear internal height 6-10 m. The top cover consists of >= 2 m loose regolith or >= 0.5 m sintered brick (see Volume II, Section 2.3.3 for details), combined with an inner PE moderation layer >= 5 mm. The annual radiation-dose target is <= 5 mSv.

Each dome is divided into two floors: the lower level contains living rooms, kitchen, dining area, and shared living spaces; the upper level contains bedrooms and private areas. Domes are connected by pressurized corridor bridges to form continuous pressurized zones that do not rely on external airlocks between buildings. A single dome houses about 20-40 residents. Once the population exceeds 200, multiple domes are linked by light rail and corridor bridges to form a "lunar village."

Surface-based habitation zones require especially strict control of lunar dust intrusion. Each dome entrance includes a two-stage airlock: the first stage is a high-pressure gas blow-off zone (>= 5 bar nitrogen, blow-off duration >= 30 s), and the second stage is an electrostatic dust-removal zone (electric field strength >= 2 kV/cm). All personnel and rovers returning from the lunar surface must be cleaned within the airlock before entering the habitation zone.

If the site is on the rim of Shackleton, the habitation system must also accommodate sloped terrain. Habitats do not use a traditional continuous foundation, but instead stand on three to four independently leveled support legs anchored into the slope with drilled anchors. Building units adopt a multi-level stacked configuration: the first level contains pressurized habitation modules and shared living areas (internal diameter 3.0-4.2 m), the second level contains dome farms and community centers, and the third level contains communication and power equipment. All buildings are covered externally with >= 2 m loose regolith or >= 0.5 m sintered brick, and internally lined with a PE moderation layer >= 5 mm, yielding annual radiation dose <= 5 mSv.

Residential platforms at different elevations are connected by elevated pressurized corridor bridges integrating power, thermal, and communication lines. In the long term, cableways may also be installed between neighboring peaks to create a fully networked three-dimensional transportation system.


#### 7.4.4 Dome-Farm Lighting: Equivalent Integrated-System Comparison of LEDs and Natural-Light Conduits

For small farms (planting area < 200 m²), LED grow lamps are the simplest solution and do not require light conduits. As farm scale grows, however, the comprehensive efficiency of natural-light conduits versus LEDs must be assessed scientifically. Farm lighting must provide both the visible light required for photosynthesis and the specific infrared and ultraviolet wavelengths needed for morphogenesis and secondary-metabolite regulation.

**(1) LED option: photovoltaic + grow-light combination**

- **Electro-optical efficiency of LED grow lights (including visible, infrared, and ultraviolet LEDs)**: about 35% overall (visible-light LEDs about 40%-50%; infrared/ultraviolet LEDs about 25%-35%; combined estimate 35%).
- **Photovoltaic conversion efficiency ($\eta_{PV}$)**: 45%.
- **Total efficiency from sunlight to light received by plants**: 45% x 35% ≈ **16%**.
- **Power demand**: About 150-200 W of total LED electrical power per square meter of planting area.

**(2) Light-conduit option: equivalent efficiency of the thermal-electric-light co-generation system**

- **Light-conduit transmission efficiency ($\eta_{pipe}$, reflective type)**: Up to 78% over 500 m.
- **Spectral splitter efficiency ($\eta_{filter}$)**: > 95%. The splitter separates visible light, which accounts for about 15% of total AM0 solar-spectrum energy, for illumination. The infrared and ultraviolet components, about 85% of total solar energy, are routed to drive supercritical CO₂ power generation. Details of the spectral-energy calculation are given in Volume III, Section 3.5.6.
- **Power-generation efficiency ($\eta_{power}$)**: 50%.
- **Equivalent efficiency assessment**: To provide 1 unit of visible light for plant illumination, the system must divert $\frac{1}{\eta_{filter} \times \eta_{pipe}}$ units of visible-light energy from the concentrated beam. If that diverted visible light were not used for illumination, it could otherwise have contributed to power generation, with a lost electric equivalent of $1 \times \eta_{power}$.

  [Note: This derivation assumes the spectral splitter is capable of fully switching the visible-light channel into the power-generation path when illumination is not needed. The switching mechanism is described in Volume III, Section 3.5.6.]

- **Therefore, the equivalent energy cost to provide 1 unit of light through the light-conduit option** = diverted light energy + lost electric equivalent.
- **Specific calculation**: To obtain 1 unit of visible light, the system must extract $\frac{1}{0.95 \times 0.78} \approx 1.35$ units of visible-light energy from the concentrated beam.
- If used for power generation instead, that 1.35 units of visible-light energy could have produced $1.35 \times 0.50 = 0.68$ units of electricity.
- Thus, the actual total energy consumed to provide 1 unit of visible light is 1.35 (light) + 0.68 (lost electricity) = 2.03 units.

- **Equivalent efficiency** = 1 / 2.03 ≈ **49%**.

**(3) Efficiency comparison conclusion**

- Total efficiency of the photovoltaic + LED option: about 16%.
- Equivalent efficiency of the light-conduit option, after accounting for lost power generation: about 49%.
- **Conclusion**: Under a comparison based on equivalent service output (light + electricity), the light-conduit option is about three times as efficient as the photovoltaic + LED option. Its core advantage is that **spectral splitting allows visible light, which photovoltaics do not convert efficiently, to be used directly for illumination, while the infrared and ultraviolet portions, which the system can use more effectively for generation, are routed into efficient power production. In short: every part of the spectrum is used for what it does best.**

**(4) Infrared and ultraviolet supplementation strategy**

- **Light-conduit option**: Light conduits transmit only visible light, so farms must also deploy **dedicated infrared and ultraviolet LED fixtures** to provide the specific wavelengths needed for morphogenesis, stress-response induction, and secondary-metabolite regulation. The power for these LEDs comes from the co-generation grid, and their energy consumption is far lower than that of a fully LED-based illumination scheme.
- **LED option**: The LED lamp arrays themselves can integrate infrared and ultraviolet chips and thus directly provide full-spectrum lighting.
- **Overall assessment**: Even after including the energy use of supplementary infrared/ultraviolet LEDs, the light-conduit option remains significantly more efficient than the all-LED option.

**(5) Economic inflection point**

Once planting area exceeds about 500 m², the one-time installation cost of a light-conduit system becomes lower than the total cost of an equivalent LED array, including fixtures, power supply, heat rejection, and electrical distribution. In addition, the light-conduit system does not consume the settlement's electrical load, freeing electricity for smelting and CNT production. It is therefore recommended that light-conduit interfaces be reserved proactively by the late industrial-outpost phase (>= 80 people), and that the industrial-town phase (>= 500 people) transition to light conduits as the primary lighting method, with LEDs retained as supplements and for photoperiod control.


### 7.5 Low-Gravity Daily-Life Support and "Earth-Like" Adaptation Design

To counter muscle atrophy and bone-density loss, astronauts and engineering personnel on long stays require daily targeted exercise. Yet for the later influx of travelers and immigrants without specialized training, daily life under low gravity presents both physiological and psychological challenges. Ordinary people cannot reasonably be expected to move by "kangaroo hopping" or sleep while strapped down. All daily-life facilities must therefore provide as much of an Earth-like experience as possible.

#### 7.5.1 Professional Training Facilities to Counter Physiological Degradation

This subsystem is intended specifically for personnel with long stays (> 3 months) to counter bone-density loss and muscle atrophy under 1/6 g. Every crew member must perform >= 1 h of compulsory exercise per day.

- **Treadmill**: Equipped with an elastic waist harness, with spring force about 5/6 of body weight to simulate Earth-gravity loading. Lunar treadmills are a mature concept already demonstrated on Earth; ISS crews use treadmills daily to counter microgravity-induced bone loss, and the required loading can be reduced proportionally for 1/6 g.
- **Resistance training frame**: Multiple sets of elastic cords, either latex or spring steel, to simulate the muscle groups trained by dumbbells and barbells. Mounted on floor and wall rails.
- **Jump-training zone**: Covered with thick foam mats (>= 30 cm thick), allowing safe vertical-jump and rolling exercises to train balance and vestibular adaptation.

#### 7.5.2 Optimization of Ordinary Walking Experience

**Principle**: Kangaroo-style hopping is energy-efficient, but it does not align with the muscle memory or social habits of terrestrial walking. The design goal is to guide users toward an alternating-step gait.

**Engineering measures**:

- **High-friction gravity-simulation floor**: Residential zones and public spaces use flexible surfaces with specially textured finishes, such as UHMWPE fabric flooring with micro-hook structures, providing a friction coefficient > 0.8.
- **Load-enhancing footwear**: All personnel wear specialized indoor shoes within habitation areas. The shoe soles do not simply add weight, because mass itself does not change the perceived walking dynamic in low gravity. Instead, the soles embed forward-leaning **elastic fiber bundles**. When lifting the foot, the bundles relax and movement feels easy; when planting the foot flat, the bundles compress, forcing the wearer to work against elastic resistance. This simulates the feeling and muscle feedback of a firm Earth-like step. The elastic bundles are designed for a fatigue life of >= 10^6 bending cycles. The actual replacement interval is defined as 80% of the measured bending life from validation item P3-H1 in Section 7.10.
- **Effect**: Through carefully engineered biomechanics, even ordinary people can be guided into a calm, Earth-like walking gait.

#### 7.5.3 Optimization of Ordinary Sleep Experience

**Principle**: Broad restraint systems analogous to racing-car harnesses would create strong feelings of confinement and unease for ordinary travelers. A gentler and more natural fixation method is required.

**Engineering measures**:

- **Flexible slow-rebound mesh sleep system**: Replaces restraint straps. The bed frame contains a slow-rebound memory-foam mesh resembling a large "dreamcatcher."
- **Experience**: After the user lies down, the mesh slowly settles to conform to the body, providing a uniform, soft enveloping sensation rather than point restraints. The mesh openings allow the arms to stretch naturally during sleep, creating a sensation closer to sleeping under a duvet, while still preventing unwanted drifting under low gravity.
- **Supplement**: Rest areas also provide weighted blankets filled with high-density fibers, delivering a pressure sensation similar to Earth's gravity and helping to relieve anxiety.

#### 7.5.4 Low-Gravity Sanitation System: Zero-Splash Hygiene Unit

Under 1/6 g, fluid behavior is dominated by surface tension. Any conventional toilet that relies on falling motion or a water seal will fail completely because of adhesion, capillary effects, and uncontrollable splash. This design therefore uses an integrated hygiene unit that combines **closed collection** with **active cleaning**.

**(a) Splash-free collection system: high-speed air-curtain vortex separator**

All traditional bowl-type forms are abandoned in favor of a sealed separation chamber with an inverted-cover geometry.

1. **Sealing and pre-pressurization**: Once the user is in position, a flexible ring in the seat automatically inflates to form a sealed contact with the body. A blower inside the chamber starts up to create negative pressure, ensuring gas flows inward only.
2. **Non-contact high-speed separation**: Two key airflow devices are arranged below the waste outlet.
   - **Annular drive air knife**: Positioned directly below the outlet, this generates a **columnar, high-speed rotating airflow**, creating a forced aerodynamic vortex.
   - **As soon as waste leaves the body, whether solid, liquid, or semi-liquid, it is immediately captured by the high-speed vortex, centrifugally accelerated, shredded, dispersed, and mixed in the air.**
   - **Wall-protection air curtain**: Along the curved inner wall of the separation chamber, a **uniform low-speed annular air curtain** is generated. All material flung outward by the vortex strikes this curtain and is reflected back into the core airflow, **ensuring that the chamber walls remain free of adhesion and splash throughout operation**.
3. **Cyclone separation and packaging**: The airflow carrying the mixed waste moves downward into a standard solid-liquid-gas three-phase cyclone separator. The separator wall is coated with an oleophobic and hydrophobic nanocoating (contact angle > 150°), and an internal scraper performs one mechanical wall-cleaning cycle after each separation run. Solids and denser liquid fractions are thrown to the inner wall and slide into a disposable biodegradable collection bag at the bottom. Under all-liquid conditions, droplets form a liquid film on the wall under centrifugal action and are collected by wall-mounted channels. Aerosol-scale fine droplets are captured by downstream demisters, either stainless-steel mesh or electrostatic coalescers, with capture efficiency >= 99%. Clean gas is deodorized, disinfected, and HEPA-filtered before being recirculated.

**(b) Integrated anal-cleaning system: air-water-knife cleaning unit**

Anal cleaning is one of the hardest user-experience problems in low-gravity sanitation. Direct water rinsing faces the same splash and adhesion problems.

1. **Protective airflow activated first**: When the cleaning head extends, it first generates a funnel-shaped outward airflow around itself, creating a one-way airflow shield in the anal region and **ensuring that any detached material or cleaning medium can only be expelled outward, never splashed inward**.
2. **Four-stage precision cleaning: air -> mist -> water -> air**
   - **Stage 1: High-pressure, high-speed air stripping**. An extremely fine, high-frequency pulsed air jet strikes the target area at very high speed, stripping and blowing away most adhered waste.
   - **Stage 2: Micron-scale mist softening and cleaning**. For residues that may have dried or bonded, a brief spray of ultra-fine disinfectant mist softens and dissolves stubborn contamination.
   - **Stage 3: Pure-water micro-mist rinse**. A pure-water mist, volume < 5 mL and droplet diameter < 50 um, dilutes residual disinfectant on the skin to below safe-threshold concentration.
   - **Stage 4: Blow-dry and fiber wipe**. High-speed air is applied again to remove the remaining droplets. Finally, a moist, slowly rotating ultra-fine fiber soft brush passes over the skin to complete the final physical wipe and ensure a clean tactile result.
3. **Self-cleaning and disinfection**: After retracting, the cleaning unit immediately enters a self-cleaning cycle. Its head is immersed in a small disinfectant chamber and then dried with internal airflow, preparing it for the next use. All material blown free during cleaning enters the cyclone separation system below.

#### 7.6.2 Integration of Recycling Equipment and Farms

CO₂ for dome farms comes from habitat air purification and industrial emissions, increasing crop photosynthetic efficiency by more than 30%. Irrigation water is reused sanitation wastewater treated through MBR + RO. Plant transpiration water is recovered by the agricultural-zone dehumidification system and returned to the drinking-water loop. Fish farming, using tilapia or carp, and vegetable cultivation form a closed aquaponics loop.

#### 7.6.3 Long-Term Nutritional Security

- **Caloric intake target**: 2,500-3,000 kcal/day for adults performing moderate physical labor. Basal metabolic rate under 1/6 g may drop by about 5%-10%, but the actual caloric requirement remains close to terrestrial levels because EVA and construction work remain physically demanding.
- **Essential micronutrients**: Calcium to counter low-gravity bone loss; vitamin D, since the lunar surface does not provide UVB and supplementation must come from diet or UVB lamps; iron, given the habitat atmosphere's roughly 30% O2 by volume and 21 kPa O2 partial pressure.
- **Emergency food reserve**: Storm shelters must store >= 30 days of complete emergency food, with caloric value >= 2,000 kcal/person/day.


### 7.7 Medical Support

#### 7.7.1 Medical-Station Configuration

| Scale | Applicable Population | Configuration | Functions |
|:-----|:-----|:-----|:-----|

| **Level II medical center** | 200-2,000 people | All Level I equipment + operating room (including anesthesia machine and shadowless lamp), X-ray / ultrasound diagnostic equipment, blood bank (autologous transfusion + frozen red-cell reserve), isolation rooms (>= 2, negative-pressure isolation, suitable for infectious-disease protection), clinical testing laboratory | Minor and medium surgery, fracture fixation, appendectomy, childbirth, infectious-disease isolation |
| **Level III hospital** | > 2,000 people | All Level II equipment + CT / MRI diagnostic equipment, intensive care unit (ICU, >= 4 beds), hemodialysis unit, rehabilitation therapy room, psychotherapy room, lunar pharmaceutical laboratory | Major surgery, multidisciplinary consultation, chronic-disease management, local drug synthesis |

#### 7.7.2 Lunar-Specific Medical Risks

- **Low-gravity adaptation injuries**: Newly arrived personnel are prone to muscle strain and balance disorders during the short adaptation period under 1/6 g. Adaptive training must begin within 48 h of arrival, and medical stations must stock adequate muscle relaxants and analgesics.
- **Elevated kidney-stone risk**: Bone decalcification accelerates under microgravity and low gravity, increasing urinary calcium concentration and thus kidney-stone risk. Medical stations must include ultrasound lithotripsy equipment and preventive hydration protocols.
- **Emergency plan for acute radiation exposure**: After SPE warning, all non-essential personnel enter storm shelters. If any crew member is accidentally exposed to an SPE high-energy particle stream during EVA, acute radiation syndrome assessment and hematopoietic growth-factor treatment must begin within 2 h.
- **Vacuum-exposure first aid**: If a crew member is briefly exposed to lunar vacuum (< 90 seconds) because of suit failure, immediate pressurized rewarming, pure-oxygen ventilation, and hyperbaric oxygen treatment are required.
- **Adaptation of medical procedures to 1/6 g**: Surgery under 1/6 g carries risks of blood splatter and organ drift. Surgical tables must therefore include negative-pressure suction and magnetic surgical tools, and both surgeon and assistant must be restrained with safety belts.


### 7.8 Staged Educational and Cultural Facilities

Educational and cultural facilities are concentrated in the **lunar-city phase (>= 1,000 people)**. Before that stage, all educational and cultural demand is met through remote Earth resources and personal devices.

#### 7.8.1 Education System (lunar-city phase)

Two to three on-site teachers provide laboratory and practical-course supervision. Laboratory equipment is shared with scientific research facilities.
- **Vocational education**: Apprenticeship training in mining, metallurgy, CNT manufacturing, and related fields, taught by senior technicians.

### 7.12 Conclusion of Volume VII

This volume completes the overall design of the lunar habitation system, raising the focus from "survival for professionals" to "daily life for ordinary citizens." Its core contributions are as follows:

1. **It establishes an equivalent integrated-system evaluation model for farm lighting.** Based on a comparison of equivalent service output, the analysis shows that the light-conduit option, with equivalent efficiency of about 49%, is markedly superior to the photovoltaic + LED option at about 16%. Its advantage comes from the spectral-splitting synergy of direct visible-light illumination paired with infrared / ultraviolet power generation. The specific infrared and ultraviolet wavelengths needed by crops are supplied by dedicated LED fixtures, and their energy use is already included in the comprehensive assessment.
2. **It enables a qualitative leap from "astronaut survival" to "ordinary human living."** By combining gravity-simulation flooring with load-enhancing footwear, it guides users toward natural walking; by replacing restraint straps with flexible slow-rebound mesh sleeping systems, it creates a secure and comfortable sleep experience. These designs substantially lower the threshold for low-gravity life and are essential if lunar cities are to attract ordinary settlers.
3. **It completely rethinks low-gravity sanitation.** The high-speed air-curtain vortex separator achieves splash-free collection, while the four-stage air-mist-water-air anal-cleaning system addresses the hardest problems of personal hygiene. The design physically eliminates splash, while explicitly accounting for the treatment of semi-liquid waste, liquid-film capture under all-liquid conditions, and residual disinfectant control.
4. **It supplements the framework with microbial-control strategy and an Earth-seed resource list.** By treating microbes as indispensable "hidden crew" within the CELSS, it defines a three-tier biosafety structure for beneficial-microbe maintenance, pathogen control, and passive base-wide monitoring, including daily rapid screening at critical nodes. It also explicitly identifies the initial one-time "seed" resources that must be delivered from Earth, including freeze-dried strains, fish fry, seeds, startup pure water, and initial aluminum ingots.
5. **It covers the full spectrum of habitation needs, from underground to surface and from bare survival to actual living.** The volume provides a fully surface-based bermed-dome option, precisely defines staged food self-sufficiency targets and the meaning of calorie self-sufficiency, and links educational and cultural facilities to urban population scale. In the validation section, testing of "Earth-like" facilities is upgraded from purely subjective assessment to combined subjective and objective metrics, and the replacement cycle and test conditions for elastic fiber bundles are given explicit engineering criteria.

### 7.13 Note on the Scope of Habitation

The content of this volume is a framework-level engineering discussion of the physical space, life-support systems, and daily-life support facilities required to sustain basic human survival under the Moon's extreme environmental conditions. It mainly covers hardware design for material cycling, radiation protection, low-gravity physiological countermeasures, and essential daily-life facilities, and therefore belongs to the domain of **survival engineering**.

The truly complete problem of human habitation, including deep psychology in long-term closed environments, the evolution of social structures, the cultural and spiritual needs of large populations, the human scale of architecture, and legal and ethical systems, is an independent and very large field that lies far beyond the scope of this appendix. Those issues must be studied separately and systematically by specialists in life sciences, social sciences, the humanities, and design. This volume serves only as the engineering-side entry point, providing the physical baseline constraints for future work.
