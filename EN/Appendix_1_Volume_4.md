###4.3.3 Helium-3 and volatile resource exploration and exploitation

** (a) Resource endowment and strategic value**

Helium-3 is one of the most important long-term strategic resources of the Moon. The helium-3 ion in the solar wind continued to be injected into the moon surface layer (at depths of about 1-3 m) over billions of years, much more efficient than the Earth because of the absence of a shield from the atmosphere and the global magnetic field. The total monthly helium-3 reserves of 1 million to 5 million tons are sufficient to support the Earth ' s clean energy needs for thousands of years. The Earth is known to have only about 0.5 tons of helium-3, so the moon surface is almost the only source of helium-3 for any fusion energy route on Earth.

It is conservatively estimated that the 3he level of the moonside is about 3.7 ppb in the highlands and about 7.8 ppb in the Moon Sea. In parts of the titanium-rich area (e.g. the Shizun Sea), 3he abundance can be inferred to be 7-18 ppb [35]. Analysis of Apollo samples showed that the lowest concentration of 3he in the Moon was about 20 ppb, making the Moon a viable 3he source [35]. Specific stock data can be found in the monthly resource analysis of section 2.5.2 of volume II of the main volume.

In addition to helium-3, the moonside granular surface is adsorbed with other volatile fractions injected by the solar wind — hydrogen (approximately 50–100 ppm), helium-4 (approximately 10–30 ppm), carbon (approximately 10–20 ppm), nitrogen (approximately 10–20 ppm) — which are simultaneously released at the time of heating to 700°C on the moon border, which can be supplemented by CNT carbon sources, propellant hydrogen, and life-support nitrogen sources, and form a complete “Moon Volatilization Resource Matrix” with volume 4.8 water ice mining and volume 5 5.2.3 carbon supply.

** (b) Exploration objectives**

The exploration objectives for helium-3 and volatilization are as follows:

- **Priority **: Moon-based areas (especially the TiO2 > 3 wt. > 3 wt. ) where helium-3 abundance is generally higher than the high-lying slope long rock area. Mare Tranquillitatis was identified as the highest titanium content (helium-3 indicator) in a region where helium-3 abundance is assumed to be 20-30 ppb [35].

##4.4 Key engineering factors affecting mines and mining operations

The challenges faced by moon mining are far greater than any mining work on Earth and are reflected mainly in the following dimensions.

# # # # 4.4.1 Impact of PSR cryogenic conditions on aqueous ice mining

Extremely low temperatures (some areas below 50 K, or approximately 223°C) in the permanent shade place stringent requirements on the normal start-up, operation and material tolerance of mining equipment [5]. Specific impacts include:

- **Lubrication failure**: Traditional lubricants are fully solidified at 50K temperature and all motor components are designed for solid lubricants (e.g. MoS2) or dry bearings.
- **Mechanism**: Conventional steel is at very low temperatures and at very low temperatures at a temperature of very much above 50K, with a potential for break-ups. Key loads are subject to the selection of low-temperature-resilient materials (e.g., aluminium, titanium or special cryogenic steel).
- **Battery performance decline**: Lithium ion batteries have decreased dramatically below -50°C, and equipment is dependent on nuclear power sources or is powered remotely from base through thermo-cable cables.
- **Thermal cycle fatigue**: The equipment is interchangeable between extremely low temperatures within PSR and the high temperatures of sunlight from day to day, with a temperature difference of more than 300°C, with very high requirements for thermal inflationary compatibility of materials.

Response strategy: Water and ice extraction equipment is based on ** presence** deployment - no more frequent movement after entering PSR and fewer heat cycles. Critical electronic equipment is placed in a temperature-conservation chamber, using RHU to maintain working temperature (>0°C). Titanium alloy (low thermal inflation factor) + ceramic coating (temperature resistant to corrosion) is used for drilling poles and heating cavities.

## 4.4.2 Moon dust environmental challenge ##

Moon dust is one of the most difficult environmental factors in moon mining. An electron lunar dust particles formed by a meteor impact (mostly < 100 μm), with sharp shape (without wind erosion) [9][10][11], with the following hazards:

-** Mechanical wear**: The sharp angles of the moon dust particles give them a high degree of grinding, and the moon dust entering the rig bearings, gears and seals will significantly accelerate the mechanical wear in a short period of time [10].
- ** Infiltration and containment**: Small particles of moon dust enable them to penetrate the gap of conventional seals, into hydraulic poles, electron circuits and electronic plugs, leading to motion cards or short circuits [11].
-** Optical pollution**: Moon dust deposition on solar panels surface will reduce photovoltaic efficiency; deposition on camera lenses and sensor windows will affect navigation and environmental perception [12].
-** Astronaut ' s health risks**: Moon dust adhesived to the surface of the lunar dress during the EVA and brought into the cabin may cause respiratory irritation upon inhalation [5].

Response strategy: All motor components are equipped with flexible dust protection ( springing energy PTFE seal) and ** multi-level maze seal** (at least level 3). Drilling wellheads are equipped with a negative pressure moon dust suction system. Solar panels and optical surfaces are equipped with a dust removal system (for the common technology, see vol. III, sect. 3.5.2 (c)). The EVA airlock sets up a moon dust cleaning station.

###4.4.3 Static adsorption and discharge

The vacuum and the strong radiation environment of the Moon makes the surface of equipment and the particles of the moon highly electrostatic [13][11][5]. Specific impacts include:

- **Additional desorption of lunar dust**: EMP is more easily adhesive to the surface of the equipment and to the space suit and conventional mechanical vibrations cannot be effectively removed.
-**The risk of electrostatic discharge**: ESD may occur when a monthly schedule accumulates a static charge at a certain threshold, causing interference or permanent damage to sensitive circuits of spacecraft, mining vehicles and electronic equipment.

Response strategy: All critical parts of equipment should be equipped with ** active electrostatic discharge route** (conductor coating or soft metal interface). High-voltage electronic equipment and communication antennas shall be equipped with ESD-protected circuits (transient voltage suppressor TVS tube + HID tube). The Moon Dust Cleaning Station uses the EOD and **ion wind and ** double models.

## 4.4.4 Energy challenges of thermal mining technologies

The core bottleneck in “thermal mining” technology, which uses direct heat heating of the Moon, is the continued supply of energy** [14] [15].

- ** Heating power density**: Very low heat coefficients (approximately 0.01-0.1 W/m.K) in a vacuum environment for ice-bearing moons, with limited heat transport from heating surfaces to deep ice-bearing layers [15].
- **Continued supply of energy**: If an electric heater is used, it must be delivered from base to PSR mining site through long-range cable (5-15 km).
-** Synergy with vol. III waste thermal recovery**: In polar sites, the waste thermal recovery programme described in vol. III, sect. 3.5.3 (d), can significantly reduce energy consumption for water ice extraction.

Impact of low gravity on fragmentation and sifting operations

** (a) Assessment of the applicability of a jaw shredder at low gravity**

The jaw shredders will face reduced charge rates and a change in the throwing movement at 1/6 gravity of the Moon. The response strategies include: an increase in the number of compulsory chargers (vibration feeders or helix transmitters, with an increase in energy consumption of about 0.5-1 kW/desk), a reduction in the eccentric axle rate (to 60-70 per cent on Earth), and a reduction in the width of the vent to increase the fragmentation ratio.

** (b) Alternative to fragmentation: shock shredder**

The use of high-speed rotation hammerhead impact material, which is less gravitational, is the primary breakout option recommended in this project [16][17]. Single power consumption is about 10-15 kW.

** (c) Spray control of shock shredders at low gravity**

In a moon-surface vacuum and 1/6 gravitational conditions, material fragments that are hit by a hammer in a shock breaker are not slowed by aerodynamic drag, are weakened by gravity constraints and have a far greater impact than the Earth. A fully constricted broken cavity design shall be used to complement the double-barrel feed and the helix transmitter with a mandatory charge [16][18]. 5 mm (aluminium alloy or CFRP) of seals, using ** spring energy PTFE material** (wielding factor 0.05-0.1, applicable temperature -250°C to +260°C). Micronegative pressure (approximately-50 Pa) is maintained in the cavity to ensure that the debris does not escape [19][18], in conjunction with negative pressure pumping.

** (d) Screening equipment selection — non-gravity dependency programme**

- ** Vibration sift - Brazil Nuts Effects Auxiliary Separatism**: DEM Simulation studies show that in the lunar microgravity environment, there are significant "Brazilian nuts effects" during vibration sifting - large particles float and small particles sink [20]. By optimizing vibration parameters and double-scave designs, sifting efficiency is about 15 per cent - 25 per cent higher than single-scave design.
- ** electrostatic wave-sifting system**: The separation of particles by particle size using electrostatic wave power without any mechanical motion components, without gravitational dependence and without air resistance in a Moon vacuum [19]. Especially for 3D print powder bed feedstock sifting and titanium concentrates, with very low energy consumption (<100 W/m2 screen) and ** completely clean.

Coarse mineral processing deployment strategy: mine vs. base

The location of deployment of rough mineral treatment (break, rough sifting) has important implications for transport costs and for the planning of the quarry. This section validates the conclusion from multiple dimensions** that the rough treatment of mines is better than base rough treatment**.

### # 4.7.1 Advantages of rough disposal of mines

** (a) Significant reduction in the quality of transport**

When the mines are coarse and sifted, a large number of useless waste stones (e.g. high-calcium stale longstones) can be dropped directly and only the substrates (<50 mm) can be returned to the base. The quality of transport can be reduced by about 30 per cent - 50 per cent, which means that the daily transport capacity of a cargo cruiser doubles.

**(b) tailings can be filled back in situ**

The waste stones from the crude sift output are filled directly in the quarry, avoiding the waste of dozens of kilometres of transport to and from unused materials.

** (c) Sealed and dissipated conditions are better**

The moon-surface vacuum is a natural thermal condensation, with shredding equipment operating in the open air in conditions that are far better than the closed space in the underground base hole segment (the latter will require additional configuration of the liquid cold circuit and the radioactive radiator plate, as described in volume III, section 3.4.3).

## # 4.7.2 should not be in mine process

Fine sifting (e.g., <100 μm class) and magnetic selection (high gradient magnetograph) require a high level of fine regulation of the supply of raw materials and equipment, and the concentrate is a high-value intermediate product, processing at the base more easily for quality control and subsequent process interfaces (direct delivery through a sealed pipeline to a catalyst extraction workshop or metal smelter).

###4.9.2 Crop selection and low dust mining options

**(a) Programme I: Microwave preburning - mechanical stripping (recommended main option)**

Recent studies at Harbin Industrial University indicate that the Moon weather layer can use focused microwaves to heat out of control to 1259°C within 800 W power and 420 seconds, and that the Moon will melt into solid-state blocks without any auxiliary heating material [24]. Based on this, the following low-dust processes are proposed:

1. **Microwave preburning**: Scanning of the moon surface layer (deep 5-10 cm) by mobile microwave generator (power 1 kW, frequency 2.45 GHz, mass 150 kg/station).
2. ** Mechanical stripping**: Crushing of the hard crust from the edges with an excavator forklift. ** Little dust is produced. **
3. **Initial fragmentation**: stripping of the symmetrical moon at the poaching site closed to 50 mm.

- ** Compared to traditional excavations**: Reduction of monthly dust emissions by more than 90 per cent.

**(b) Option II: Exposure pressure-freeze-takeout (optional referral)**

The Act completely circumvents traditional mining methods, such as cutting and drilling, and has the unique advantages of small-scale dust, zero cutting and very simple equipment.

** Rationale and workflow**

1. **Back to **: The mechanical arm places the pressure plate on the moon surface. The outer edge of the plate is a wedge blade (30°-45°, WC-Co hard alloy, hard 86 HRA - see section 4.5.3), under pressure (approximately 0.5-1 kN/m2). The entry resistance of the loose Moon is extremely low, and the blades enter directly into the 5-10 cm depth of the dry Moon under pressure and form a closed cavity. ** The process was completed entirely between the contact areas without creating any dust. **
2. **Plution freeze**: injection of liquid water into the closed cavity by microholes on the pressure plate. Water is not exposed to a vacuum during injection and liquids are maintained to penetrate the moon. Upon completion of the injection, the water is frozen rapidly in a low-stemperature (approximately -180°C) and vacuum environment, placing loose granulate glue into a tundra layer, while the pressure plate is frozen firmly with the freezer.
3. ** The whole block is taken up**: The mechanical arm is removed directly from the pressure plate along with the frozen earth and moved to the top of the transport container.
4. ** Unfrozen separation**: When transported to the base, the third volume of low-temperature waste heat (30-50°C) is used in a closed cavity to heat up the thaw. After the ice melts, the pressure plate is automatically removed and returns to repeat use in the mining field. Melting water is recovered through the sink, with a recycling utilization target of 80 per cent.

** Equipment and energy consumption parameters**

- Pressure plate size: 1 m x 1 m, single plate mass ≤50 kg (aluminium alloy plate + WC-Co blade mouth + injection pump + heating component).
- Pressure plate power: about 2 kW (including aqueous pumps and heating elements).
- Maximum amount of water: about 12.5 L/m2 (freeze 5 cm thick, filled with about 50 per cent of the pore of the Moon) and 80 per cent of the circulation utilization, with approximately 2.5 L/m2 per round.
- ** Compared to traditional excavations**:** Zero dust, Zero cutting**, the pressure plate itself is a triad of cutting, watering and lifting.

** (c) Pre-treatment of bulk particles**

Both low-dust mining programmes have effective operational depths of 5-10 cm. Post-operational residuals of >10 cm in diameter (extracted particles) shall be removed before the next round. Handled using a self-propelled torque.

# # # 4.9.3 Self-propelled torque

** Rationale**

A small self-propelled tracked platform (mass 650 kg) carrying a 6-degree hydraulic arm. The end of the mechanical arm is equipped with a fast-changing tool interface, which automatically replaces the following two headers:

1. ** Hydraulic clamps**: maximum opening 50 cm, material WC-Co hard alloy (rigid 86 HRA), combined with power 5 kN. With a rock of 10 to 50 cm in diameter, it is placed in a container seal, which follows the transporter, and is transported to a central break point on the edge of the mine. The whole process was dustless.
2. ** Sealed crack mask**: aluminium alloy or CFRP material, wall thickness 3 mm. PTFE seals are installed at the bottom of the spring, which form a temporary seal when pressing the moon watch. Mask integration:
- ** Hydraulic head** (main scenario): WC-Co hard alloy, push ≥50 kN, for rock with medium hardness, morphological rules.
- ** Small shock hammer** (back-up scenario): 200 J/s for rock with irregular shapes and difficult to reach with a head pressure.
The same hydraulic circuit is shared between the two modes of execution, with the mechanical arm control system being autonomously selected on the basis of rock form and hardness.

** Distinguishing logic**: 3-D scanning of target rocks by a mechanized end-of-arms 3-D visual camera. If the rock is 20 cm in diameter and morphological rules -- it's taken directly with a clamp. If the rock is >20 cm in diameter, or in an irregular form, or is embedded in the underlying rock — drops a sealed crack and chooses to crush or crush the hammer according to the form. The material remains in place after the sealed breakup as a ground level filler for low-dust stripping.

** Key parameters**:

- ** Walking institutions**: small tracked chassis, power approximately 2 kW. Batteries and autonomous navigation systems are shared with surveyors.
- ** Hydraulic mechanical arm**: 6 degrees of freedom, maximum stretch radius 2 m. Maximum load load 500 kg (under moon gravitational). Switch interface hydraulic drive, 30 seconds switch. Power is about 5 kW.
- **Battery capacity**: ≥60 kwh, single charge for approximately 6-10 hours.
- ** Working temperature**: system-wide adaptation - 180°C to 200°C. Sealed PTFE, with cryogenic silicon oil or NK liquid metal.

** Deployment programme**: two full-scale mine deployments for the second capital phase, for the construction of the bone and metal and mineral mining fields. Each unit is equipped with a small transport vehicle (two tons dwt, 50 kWh Battery, approximately eight hours) to receive the rock extracted from the clamp and transport it to a centralized fragmentation point.

# 4.14, end of volume 4

The present volume completes the planning for the exploration and mining of the lunar industrial resources — from the systematic combing of mineral data from candidate sites, analysis of the full extent of the impact of the extremes of the Moon on mining operations, to the phased programme of work for water ice mining, building bone extraction, metal mineral selection and helium-3 and volatilization, covering the full range of raw material requirements from a permanent base, from “liveable” to “manufactible”.

** Core conclusion:**

1. **The extraction of water ice must be gradual in stages and energy strategies determine the cost of mining. ** Phases 0 to 1 are dominated by independent electric heating (50 kWh/kg per unit electricity consumption) and phase 2 is replaced by after-heat recovery at power plants (Target 30 kWh/kg). The evolution of this energy strategy is fully in line with the pace of power construction for volume III of the Base.

2. ** The completion of rough processing of minerals at the mine is a better engineering option. ** In situ fragmentation of mines can reduce the quality of transport by 30 per cent - 50 per cent, and natural monthly vacuum heat conditions are better than underground base sealing. Fine sifting and magnetic selection are still recommended at the Base to facilitate quality control and subsequent process alignment.

3. ** The low-dust mining programme consists of two optional zero-dust paths and a large-particle pre-treatment unit. ** Microwave pre-combination - mechanical stripping increases the moon surface to 1,100°C so that it is condensed, the contact surface is pressured into-freeze-extracted and the whole process is closed and the dust is cut to zero. For the remaining bulk particles, the self-repeated ply-crumbs and sealed shredding functions are integrated and the most appropriate treatment is available on-site.

4. **Helium-3 and volatilizers are formally included in exploration and exploitation planning as a fourth category of resources. ** The Moon Sea basalt cover area (especially the Silent Sea high titanium area) is a priority exploration objective and the storage target 100 t. The extraction programme uses a step-by-step process of “sealed heating-gas separation-tail-brick construction” to achieve solid waste-free emissions. An industrial feasibility reference [38][39] is provided for this programme by the Interlune and Vermeer moon excavator prototype and NASA Prospect Moon payload (launched 2028).

5. **The problem of flashing in a moon-surface vacuum and low gravity must be addressed through a closed design. ** Full closed broken cavity + double-barrel feeder + mandatory charge + negative pressure suction with a spring-coated PTFE.

6. ** The work temperature range and hardness requirements for all key performance components have been consolidated in Section 4.5. ** The drilling, fragmentation, cutting and sealing equipment is designed according to the harshest conditions (-250°C to 200°C).

7. ** Energy consumption, renewal and launch quality of mine-wide equipment have been paid for. ** The total daily consumption of the whole mine in phase 1 is approximately 1,036 kWh and the total mass of equipment is about 19.8 tons (net mass). Battery-driven equipment is renewed for 6-10 hours. These data will directly support the accounting of transport system costs in volume X of the economic model.

8.** The scarcity of mineral data remains the greatest uncertainty. ** Mineral data from the Philolaus impact crater are almost zero and must be filled in volume I task cluster II/III.
