### 4.1 Introductory Notes

This volume defines the material basis and raw-material assurance of the lunar industrial system, focused on answering the following core questions:

> After a permanent base is built, where does water come from? Where do construction aggregates come from? Where do CNT-factory catalysts and ores for metal smelting come from? Under extreme lunar conditions, how can these raw resources be extracted from the surface and transported to the base with minimal energy and maximum efficiency? What is each device's energy consumption? How long can battery-powered equipment run continuously? How can hardness and service life of key executive components such as drilling, crushing, and cutting be guaranteed under thermal shock? Can we design a fundamentally cut-free, low-dust method that relies only on static pressure and the bonding force of ice to strip regolith? What is the total mass of all equipment that must be launched from Earth? How large are helium-3 reserves in regolith, and how can this long-term fusion fuel be extracted?

This volume builds on:
- **Volume 1 (Pioneer Missions)**: Confirmed extractable polar water ice (P0-M1), regolith reserves for construction feedstock (P0-M7), and regolith reserves for catalyst feedstock (P0-M8).
- **Volume 2 (Site Selection and Infrastructure)**: Established resource-access constraints for recommended sites (ice fields 5-15 km from base; construction and catalyst ore zones within rover radius), plus phased adaptation plans for rugged terrain.
- **Volume 3 (Energy System)**: Provided medium/low-temperature and high-temperature process heat interfaces for ice extraction and metal smelting.

Core tasks of this volume:

1. Systematically consolidate known mineral data near candidate sites to provide quantitative resource evidence for final site selection.
2. Define exploration and reserve-assessment criteria for four major resource classes needed for lunar industrialization: water ice, construction aggregate, metal and catalyst feedstock, and helium-3 plus volatiles.
3. Analyze comprehensive impacts of the lunar extreme environment on mining operations, including PSR cryogenic temperatures, dust wear, electrostatic adsorption, and low-gravity effects on crushing/screening and material ejecta.
4. Design a phased engineering plan for water-ice extraction: from stand-alone electric heating in early infrastructure phases to power-plant waste-heat recovery in industrial phases. All equipment includes key execution parameters such as operating temperature range, hardness, and material.
5. Propose exploration targets and conceptual extraction plans for helium-3 and volatiles to realize staged utilization: "extract helium first, then make bricks."
6. Provide mass, energy-scale estimates, and endurance evaluations for all mining equipment and facilities. Equipment mass directly determines Earth-launch transport cost and must be estimated at design stage.
7. Determine rational deployment of coarse mineral processing (crushing/screening), mine-site processing vs base processing, via integrated optimization of transport cost, sealed heat-rejection conditions, and phased implementation strategy.
8. Design low-dust mining schemes for construction aggregate and metal minerals, including microwave pre-sintering + mechanical stripping and contact-interface pressing + freeze-lift methods. For residual large particles, design a self-propelled clamp-crush integrated unit as supporting equipment.

### 4.2 Known Mineral Data Near Candidate Sites

Before large-scale mining starts, existing mineral data near candidate sites must be systematically reviewed. The following integrates global lunar-orbit remote-sensing results and sample analyses.

#### 4.2.1 Shackleton Crater and Surrounding Area

**(a) Mineral data maturity: High**

The Shackleton-de Gerlache area is currently one of the global focal points of lunar resource prospecting. A comprehensive site-feasibility study published in 2026 evaluated nine candidate mining sites in the Shackleton south-polar region, systematically analyzing geologic features, water-ice potential, terrain, and engineering feasibility, and providing first-generation engineering-grade data for ore-body modeling in the Shackleton area [1].

**(b) Resource characteristics**

- **Water-ice resources**: PSRs inside Shackleton crater have been confirmed by the LRO neutron spectrometer and LCROSS impact experiment to contain substantial water ice [2][3]. Area with water-equivalent hydrogen content H > 2 wt% is preliminarily estimated to be no less than 1 km2.
- **Construction aggregate and metal minerals**: Geological sections on the Shackleton rim are dominated by highland anorthosite, interbedded with breccia layers formed by impact ejecta. The area **lacks** broad mare-basalt cover. Anorthite content can exceed 50% (meeting construction-feedstock criteria), but iron-titanium oxide content is low (<5%), so this is **not** a high-ilmenite mare-basalt distribution zone. Catalyst feedstock and metal-smelting ore must be sourced from more distant mare-basalt regions [4].
- **Topography**: The Shackleton crater rim is steep; many areas exceed 15 degrees slope, limiting deployment of large mining equipment [5].

**(c) Nearest high-ilmenite zones**

The nearest known high-ilmenite zones to Shackleton are several small mare-basalt patches along the SPA Basin margin. Based on Kaguya data, JAXA identified 51 ilmenite-enriched zones on the lunar nearside [6]. Latest estimates place total lunar-surface ilmenite resources at approximately 150 trillion tons, mainly hosted in mare-basalt strata [7].

#### 4.2.2 Philolaus Crater

**(a) Mineral data maturity: Extremely low**

In currently public data, **no dedicated studies** have been found on extractable mineral reserves in the Philolaus crater region. Mineral prospecting there depends entirely on field data from Mission Groups 1-3 in Volume 1.

#### 4.2.3 Marius Hills

**(a) Mineral data maturity: Moderate (macroscopic)**

Marius Hills lies on the edge of Oceanus Procellarum on the lunar nearside, and Oceanus Procellarum is one of the globally highest-known ilmenite-content mare-basalt regions. However, **high-resolution mineral mapping specifically for Marius Hills is still very limited**, and has not yet reached engineering reserve precision at the individual mine scale.

#### 4.2.4 Mare Tranquillitatis "Skylight"

**(a) Mineral data maturity: Moderate (macroscopic)**

Mare Tranquillitatis is listed by ESA as a high-priority candidate area [8]. Mare basalt is widespread with rich ilmenite content; many studies identify it as one of the highest Ti-enrichment regions on the nearside. Its equatorial location, however, means zero water-ice resources.

### 4.3 Resource Exploration and Reserve Assessment

#### 4.3.1 Exploration Targets for Three Major Resource Classes

Key resources needed for lunar industrialization are prioritized into three classes:

**(a) Water ice - the cornerstone of survival and propellant**

- Occurrence: ice-bearing regolith in PSRs, with water-equivalent hydrogen content H > 2 wt%.
- Exploration target: on top of water-rich zones confirmed in Volume 1, increase drill density and improve reserve precision from +/-30% (2sigma) to +/-15%.
- Key parameters: thickness distribution of ice-bearing regolith, ice-soil mixing ratio, average thickness of overlying dry layer.
- Uses: life-support water, hydrogen/oxygen production (propellant), and water supply for hydrogen energy storage systems.

**(b) Construction aggregate - the skeleton of infrastructure**

- Occurrence: high-quality construction-feedstock regolith meeting Volume 1 Section 1.3 criterion (b): anorthite >=50% + iron-titanium oxides 10%-20% + bimodal gradation.
- Exploration target: identify at least 3 independent quarry sites within 10 km of the base, with total mineable reserves >=10^6 m3.
- Key parameters: loose-layer thickness, bedrock burial depth, spatial variation coefficient of particle-size gradation.
- Uses: sintered regolith bricks, regolith concrete, road paving, and radiation-shield cover.

**(c) Metal minerals and catalyst feedstock - the bloodstream of industry**

- Occurrence: ilmenite-rich mare basalt meeting Volume 1 Section 1.3 criterion (c): ilmenite >=10 wt% + natural Fe-Ni particles >=0.5 wt%.
- Exploration target: identify at least 2 independent mine sites within 10 km of base, with total mineable reserves >=10^5 t.
- Key parameters: ilmenite particle size (must be >=50 um for magnetic separation), pyroxene Fe content (>=10 wt% FeO), average overburden thickness.
- Uses: CNT catalysts (Fe, Ni), and smelting feedstock for structural metals (Al, Fe, Ti).

#### 4.3.3 Exploration and Extraction of Helium-3 and Volatiles

**(a) Resource endowment and strategic value**

Helium-3 is one of the Moon's most important long-term strategic resources. Solar-wind helium-3 ions have been implanted into the regolith surface layer (depth about 1-3 m) for billions of years. Because the Moon lacks an atmosphere and global magnetic shielding, implantation efficiency is much higher than on Earth. Total lunar helium-3 reserves are estimated at 1-5 million tons, enough to support thousands of years of clean-energy demand on Earth. Known terrestrial helium-3 reserves are only about 0.5 tons, making the Moon the near-unique source for helium-3 required by any fusion-energy pathway.

Conservative estimates put ³He content at about 3.7 ppb in highlands and 7.8 ppb in maria. In some titanium-rich regions (such as Mare Tranquillitatis), ³He abundance may reach 7-18 ppb [35]. Apollo sample analyses indicate a minimum ³He concentration around 20 ppb in regolith, making regolith a viable ³He source [35]. Detailed reserve data is in Main Volume 2 Section 2.5.2.

Beyond helium-3, regolith particle surfaces adsorb other solar-wind volatiles: hydrogen (~50-100 ppm), helium-4 (~10-30 ppm), carbon (~10-20 ppm), nitrogen (~10-20 ppm). These are co-released when regolith is heated to 700C, and can serve as supplementary CNT carbon feedstock, supplementary propellant hydrogen, and supplementary life-support nitrogen source. Together with water-ice extraction in Volume 4 Section 4.8 and carbon-feedstock supply in Volume 5 Section 5.2.3, they form a complete "lunar volatile resource matrix."

**(b) Exploration targets**

Using the three-class resource criteria in Volume 4 Section 4.3.1 as reference, exploration targets for helium-3 and volatiles are:

- **Priority areas**: mare-basalt regions, especially high-Ti basalts with TiO2 > 3 wt%, where regolith helium-3 abundance is generally higher than in highland anorthosite regions. Mare Tranquillitatis has been identified as the highest Ti region (an indicator for helium-3), with inferred enriched zones at 20-30 ppb [35].
- **Proven reserve target**: delineate at least one priority extraction zone with helium-3 reserves >=100 t (equivalent to total volatile reserves >=1,000 t hydrogen equivalent), area >=1 km2, and average regolith thickness >=2 m.
- **Sampling validation**: validate vertical helium-3 abundance distribution (typical solar-wind implantation profile: highest near surface and exponentially decaying with depth) via in-situ heating-mass spectrometry by Mission Group 2 and deep-core sampling by Mission Group 3 (drill depth >=3 m). Existing international research has proposed nondestructive in-situ ³He exploration using RF atomic magnetometers, detecting roughly 5 ppb ³He in ~2 g regolith samples within about 5 minutes, offering a practical reference for payload design in this mission set [36].

**(c) Conceptual extraction plan**

Helium-3 extraction is a typical large-scale, low-grade, high-energy process. The HEAT prototype has verified that heating regolith simulant (<100 um) to 700C can release ³He plus volatiles including H₂, ⁴He, CO₂, CH₄, N₂, and H₂O [37]. Order-of-magnitude estimate of the conceptual plan:

**Feedstock**: mare-basalt regolith with ~20 ppb helium-3. Processing one ton of regolith yields about 0.02 g helium-3 (assuming 70% recovery).

**Process flow**:
1. **Regolith collection**: performed by the construction-aggregate excavation equipment (medium excavator rover) described in Volume 4 Section 4.9.1, continuously mining surface regolith (0-2 m) in priority helium-3 zones. Interlune and Vermeer Corporation have built a full-scale lunar helium-3 excavator prototype with processing capacity up to 100 t/h and annual target output around 20 kg helium-3 [38]. Monthly throughput target is from 10^4-ton scale (early) to 10^6-ton scale (long-term).
2. **Sealed heating**: regolith enters a sealed heating chamber and is heated to ~700C using high-temperature steam extraction (500-750C) from the power plant in Volume 3 Section 3.5.7 or via electric heating. At this temperature, solar-wind volatiles (H₂, ⁴He, ³He, CH₄, N₂, CO₂) are released within ~10-30 minutes. In 2026 NASA awarded Interlune a $6.9M contract to develop Prospect Moon payloads for in-situ lunar regolith heating and direct volatile-gas measurement (including helium-3 and hydrogen), expected launch in 2028 - the first in-situ extraction of solar-wind volatiles on the Moon [39]. Heat exchangers are also considered to optimize particle flow and heat-transfer efficiency [37]. Volatile recovery target >=90% (reference Volume 4 reference [19]).
3. **Gas separation and purification**: after dewatering by condensation, mixed gas is progressively separated by combined cryogenic distillation and membrane separation into hydrogen (reducing agent and propellant), helium-4 (cooling medium and welding shielding gas), hydrocarbons (CNT carbon source), nitrogen (life-support supplement), and finally concentrated helium-3 product. Isotope separation (³He/⁴He natural ratio ~3x10^-4) is the most technology-intensive step.
4. **Tailings handling**: heated extraction residue is cooled and fed directly into building-material lines in Volume 5 Section 5.5 (sintered bricks or concrete feedstock), achieving staged utilization "extract helium first, then make bricks" with zero solid-waste discharge.

**Energy-scale estimate**: full-process unit consumption per ton regolith is roughly 500-1,000 kWh thermal (to 700C) plus 100-200 kWh electric (gas separation and purification). At 70% helium-3 recovery and 20 ppb grade, extracting 1 g helium-3 requires processing ~70,000 tons regolith, with total process energy around 42-84 GWh. For a 100 MWe fusion plant assuming annual D-³He consumption of ~100 kg helium-3, supporting fuel supply would require processing ~7 million tons regolith per year, with associated mining/heating power demand around 500-1,000 MWe - the same order as plant output itself (actual gain depends on plant Q value).

Therefore, commercialization trigger conditions for helium-3 extraction are: (1) full build-out of lunar heavy-industry infrastructure (mid-Phase IV), (2) engineering breakthrough in D-³He fusion, and (3) lunar installed power reaching gigawatt scale, enabling positive feedback: "use lunar power to mine helium-3, then use helium-3 fusion to supply more power." Before that, helium-3 extraction operates only for technology verification and limited stockpiling.

#### 4.3.4 Independent Engineering Pathway for Asteroid Carbon Source

##### (1) Design Principles

Asteroid carbon sources, as the long-term primary carbon source for lunar industry, are obtained and transported **completely independently of any other infrastructure on Earth or in cislunar space**. The engineering pathway described in this section does not depend on any external mega-structures, using only spacecraft and equipment deployable by lunar industry itself.

##### (2) Carbon-Source Asteroid Selection and Capture

| Parameter | Design Value | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Target asteroid type | C-type (carbonaceous chondrite) | Carbon content >=3 wt%, water content >=5 wt% |
| Target size | Diameter 10-50 m | Mass ~1,000-100,000 t; single capture provides centuries of carbon supply |
| Orbit selection | Near-Earth asteroids (NEA) | Earth-crossing orbits, Δv requirement <=5 km/s |
| Capture method | Inflatable capture bag (TransAstra technology) or surface anchor + towing | Capture bag technology validated on ISS (1-m scale) |
| Tug type | Nuclear-thermal/electric-propulsion space tug (same type as Main Volume 2 Section 2.5.6) | Isp >=1,500 s, thrust >=10 N, mass <=10 t/unit |
| Target tow orbit | Earth-Moon L1 point or high lunar orbit (altitude >=1,000 km from surface) | Facilitates subsequent in-orbit processing |

##### (3) Towing Plan and Energy Consumption

A single mission tows one C-type asteroid with diameter 50 m (estimated mass ~100,000 tons) to the Earth-Moon L1 point. Uses nuclear-thermal propulsion (Isp = 900 s) or electric propulsion (Isp = 3,000 s). For electric propulsion:

- Asteroid mass: $m = 1\times10^8\text{ kg}$
- Δv required (NEA → Earth-Moon L1): ~2.5 km/s
- Propellant mass (electric propulsion, Isp = 3,000 s): $m_{\text{prop}} = m \cdot (e^{\Delta v/(I_{sp}g_0)} - 1) \approx 1\times10^8 \times (e^{2500/(29430)} - 1) \approx 1\times10^8 \times 0.089 \approx 8.9\times10^6\text{ kg}$ (~8,900 t)
- Single-mission propellant requirement for tug is ~9,000 t, producible from hydrogen/oxygen via water-ice electrolysis on the lunar surface

##### (4) In-Orbit Pyrolysis and Carbon Source Extraction

After the asteroid is towed to Earth-Moon L1 or high lunar orbit, it is not landed on the lunar surface; instead, it undergoes in-orbit pyrolysis processing.

### 4.4 Key Engineering Factors Affecting Mines and Mining Operations

Lunar mining faces challenges beyond any terrestrial mining project, mainly at the following levels.

#### 4.4.1 Impacts of PSR Cryogenic Conditions on Water-Ice Mining

Extreme low temperatures inside PSRs (below 50 K in some areas, approximately -223C) impose strict requirements on startup, operation, and material tolerance for mining equipment [5]. Specific impacts include:

- **Lubrication failure**: conventional lubricants fully solidify at 50 K; all moving parts must use solid lubricants (such as MoS2) or dry-bearing designs.
- **Material embrittlement**: ductile-brittle transition temperatures of ordinary steels are far above 50 K, risking brittle fracture. Critical load-bearing parts must use cryogenic-toughness materials (e.g., Al alloys, Ti alloys, or special cryogenic steels).
- **Battery performance degradation**: Li-ion battery capacity drops sharply below -50C; equipment should rely on nuclear power sources or insulated-cable remote power from base.
- **Thermal cycling fatigue**: repeated switching between deep PSR cryogenic temperatures and sunlit high temperatures can exceed 300C temperature differential, imposing stringent thermal-expansion compatibility requirements.

Mitigation strategy: water-ice excavation equipment is primarily **stationary-deployed** - after entering PSR, avoid frequent relocation to reduce thermal cycles. Key electronics are enclosed in insulated sealed cabins and kept above 0C by RHUs. Drill strings and heating chambers use Ti alloy (low thermal expansion) + ceramic coatings (temperature and corrosion resistance).

#### 4.4.2 Lunar Dust Challenges

Lunar dust is one of the most difficult environmental factors in lunar mining. Charged impact-generated dust has fine particle size (mostly <100 um) and sharp morphology (no aeolian rounding) [9][10][11], with these hazards:

- **Mechanical wear**: sharp-edged dust particles are highly abrasive; dust entering drill bearings, gears, and seals can rapidly accelerate wear [10].
- **Penetration and blockage**: tiny particles can penetrate gaps in standard seals and enter hydraulic rods, motor windings, and electrical connectors, causing jamming or short circuits [11].
- **Optical contamination**: dust deposition on solar panels reduces PV efficiency; deposition on camera lenses and sensor windows impairs navigation and perception [12].
- **Astronaut health risk**: during EVA, dust adheres to suit surfaces and is brought into habitats; inhalation may irritate the respiratory system [5].

Mitigation strategy: all moving parts use elastic dust boots (spring-energized PTFE seals) and **multi-stage labyrinth seals** (at least three stages). Drillheads use negative-pressure dust extraction at bore entrance. Solar panels and optical surfaces use electrodynamic dust shields (technology shared with Volume 3 Section 3.5.2(c)). EVA airlocks include dust-cleaning stations.

#### 4.4.3 Electrostatic Adhesion and Discharge

Vacuum and strong radiation make equipment surfaces and dust particles prone to static-charge accumulation [13][11][5]. Impacts include:

- **Increased dust adhesion**: charged dust adheres more strongly to equipment and suits; conventional mechanical vibration cannot effectively remove it.
- **ESD risk**: when accumulated surface charge reaches threshold, electrostatic discharge may interfere with or permanently damage sensitive circuits of spacecraft, mining vehicles, and electronics.

Mitigation strategy: all critical equipment locations require **active electrostatic discharge paths** (conductive coatings or flexible metallic grounding straps). High-voltage electronics and communication antennas require ESD protection circuits (TVS diodes + gas discharge tubes). Dust-cleaning stations use dual-mode electrodynamic curtain plus **ion-wind neutralization**.

#### 4.4.4 Energy Challenges of Thermal Mining

"Thermal mining" directly heats regolith to sublimate water ice; the core bottleneck is **continuous energy supply** [14][15].

- **Heating power density**: thermal conductivity of ice-bearing regolith in vacuum is very low (~0.01-0.1 W/m.K), so heat conduction from heater surface to deeper ice-bearing layers is limited [15].
- **Sustained energy supply**: with electric heaters, long-distance cable transmission (5-15 km) from base to PSR mine site is required.
- **Synergy with Volume 3 waste-heat recovery**: in polar siting, waste-heat recovery in Volume 3 Section 3.5.3(d) can significantly reduce water-ice extraction energy consumption.

#### 4.4.5 Terrain and Accessibility Constraints

- **Shackleton crater rim**: hazardous terrain with many slopes above 15 degrees. Transport-route planning and construction must fully leverage rugged-terrain adaptation schemes in Volume 2 Section 2.3.2(d) [5].
- **Marius Hills and Mare Tranquillitatis skylights**: terrain is relatively gentle in mare-basalt regions. Vertical transport near skylights may become a logistics bottleneck.

#### 4.4.6 Effects of Low Gravity on Crushing and Screening

**(a) Suitability of jaw crushers under low gravity**

Under lunar 1/6 gravity, jaw crushers face reduced discharge rate and altered projectile motion. Mitigations include adding forced-discharge mechanisms (vibratory feeders or screw conveyors, additional ~0.5-1 kW per unit), lowering eccentric-shaft speed (to 60%-70% of Earth operation), and narrowing discharge opening to increase crushing ratio.

**(b) Alternative crushing scheme: impact crushers**

Impact crushing using high-speed rotating hammers depends less on gravity and is the recommended primary-crushing scheme [16][17]. Single-unit energy consumption is about 10-15 kW.

**(c) Ejecta control for impact crushers under low gravity**

In lunar vacuum and 1/6 gravity, fragments struck by hammers do not decelerate by aerodynamic drag, and gravity constraint is weaker, so splash range is much larger than on Earth. Fully sealed crushing-chamber design is required, with dual-layer airlock feed and screw-conveyor forced discharge [16][18]. Seal-housing wall thickness >=5 mm (Al alloy or CFRP). Seals use **spring-energized PTFE** (friction coefficient 0.05-0.1, operating range -250C to +260C). Maintain slight negative pressure in chamber (~-50 Pa), combined with suction extraction, to prevent fragment escape [19][18].

**(d) Screening equipment selection - gravity-independent schemes**

- **Vibratory screening aided by Brazil Nut Effect**: DEM studies show significant BNE under lunar microgravity, with coarse particles rising and fine particles sinking [20]. With optimized vibration parameters and dual-layer screen design, screening efficiency can improve by about 15%-25% over single-layer design.
- **Electrostatic traveling-wave screening**: uses electrostatic traveling-wave force to separate particles by size, with no mechanical moving parts and no gravity dependence; in lunar vacuum there is no air-drag interference [19]. Especially suitable for fine classification of feedstocks for 3D-print powder beds and ilmenite concentrate, with very low power (<100 W/m2 screen area) and **zero dust generation**.

### 4.7 Deployment Strategy for Coarse Mineral Processing: Mine Site vs Base

Deployment location for coarse mineral processing (crushing/coarse screening) significantly affects transport cost and mine planning. This section demonstrates why **mine-site coarse processing is superior to base-side coarse processing**.

#### 4.7.1 Advantages of Mine-Site Coarse Processing

**(a) Major reduction in transport mass**

After on-site coarse crushing/screening, large amounts of useless coarse waste rock (e.g., highland anorthosite blocks below anorthite threshold) can be discarded at source, and only undersize fractions (<50 mm) are sent to base. Transported mass can be reduced by about 30%-50%, effectively doubling daily transport capacity of a cargo rover.

**(b) Tailings can be backfilled on site**

Coarse-screen rejects are backfilled directly in the pit, avoiding multi-kilometer round-trip transport of useless material.

**(c) Better sealing and thermal conditions**

Lunar vacuum is a natural heat sink. Surface operation offers much better heat rejection for crushing equipment than enclosed underground base segments (which require additional liquid-cooling loops and radiator panels, as in Volume 3 Section 3.4.3).

#### 4.7.2 Processes Not Suitable for Mine-Site Placement

Fine screening (e.g., electrostatic screening for <100 um fractions) and magnetic separation (high-gradient separators) require stable feed and precise equipment tuning. Concentrates are high-value intermediates; base-side processing is better for quality control and downstream integration (direct sealed-pipeline transfer to catalyst extraction workshops or metal smelters).

#### 4.9.2 Pit Selection and Low-Dust Mining Schemes

**(a) Scheme 1: Microwave pre-sintering + mechanical stripping (recommended primary scheme)**

Latest research from Harbin Institute of Technology shows that lunar weathered layer can be heated by focused microwave at 800 W for 420 s to thermal-runaway temperatures up to 1259C, melting regolith into solid blocks without any auxiliary heating consumables [24]. Based on this, the following low-dust process is proposed:

1. **Microwave pre-sintering**: use mobile microwave generators (>=1 kW, 2.45 GHz, <=150 kg/unit) to scan-irradiate surface regolith layer (depth 5-10 cm).
2. **Mechanical stripping**: excavator rover bucket pries up sintered hard crust from edges, removing it in blocks. **Almost no dust generation.**
3. **Primary crushing**: stripped block regolith is crushed on site by sealed impact crusher to <=50 mm.

- **Compared with conventional excavation**: dust emission reduced by >90%.

**(b) Scheme 2: Contact-interface press-in, freeze, and lift (alternative recommended scheme)**

This method completely avoids conventional cutting and drilling, with unique advantages of zero dust, zero cutting, and simplified equipment.

**Working principle and workflow**

1. **Plate placement and insertion**: robotic arm places press-in plate onto regolith surface. Plate perimeter is wedge edge (30-45 degree edge angle, WC-Co carbide, hardness >=86 HRA, see Section 4.5.3), with downforce (~0.5-1 kN/m2). Penetration resistance of loose dry regolith is very low; the edge penetrates directly 5-10 cm to form a closed cavity. **This process is fully confined at the contact interface and produces no dust.**
2. **Water injection and freezing**: liquid water is injected through micropores on the plate into the closed cavity. Water remains liquid during injection because it is not exposed to vacuum, infiltrating pore space. After injection, low lunar temperature (~-180C) and vacuum rapidly freeze water, cementing loose particles into a frozen-soil layer and firmly freezing the press-in plate to the frozen block.
3. **Whole-block lifting**: robotic arm lifts plate and frozen block together and moves them over transport container.
4. **Thaw and separation**: after transport to base, low-temperature waste heat (30-50C) from Volume 3 is used in a sealed chamber to thaw the frozen block. When ice melts, the plate detaches automatically and is reused. Meltwater is recovered through a collection trough, target recycling >=80%.

**Equipment and energy parameters**

- Plate size: 1 m x 1 m; single-plate mass <=50 kg (Al body + WC-Co blade + water pump + heater).
- Plate power: ~2 kW (including water pump and heater).
- Upper-limit water usage: ~12.5 L/m2 (5 cm frozen-soil thickness, about 50% pore filling), recycling >=80%, make-up water ~2.5 L/m2 per cycle.
- **Compared with conventional excavation**: **zero dust, zero cutting**. The plate itself integrates cutting, water injection, and lifting.

**(c) Pretreatment for large particles**

Effective operating depth of both low-dust schemes is 5-10 cm. Residual rocks >10 cm diameter must be removed before the next cycle using a self-propelled clamp-crush integrated unit.

#### 4.9.3 Self-Propelled Clamp-Crush Integrated Unit

**Working principle**

A small self-propelled tracked platform (mass <=650 kg) carries a 6-DOF hydraulic robotic arm. The end effector has quick-swap tooling that switches between:

1. **Hydraulic clamp**: max jaw opening 50 cm, WC-Co carbide jaws (hardness >=86 HRA), clamping force >=5 kN. Grips rocks of 10-50 cm diameter and places them into sealed hopper of follower transport cart for transfer to centralized crushing point at pit edge. Entire process is dust-free.
2. **Sealed crushing hood**: Al alloy or CFRP, wall thickness >=3 mm. Bottom uses spring-energized PTFE seals to form temporary seal when pressed to surface. Inside hood:
   - **Hydraulic press head** (primary): WC-Co carbide, thrust >=50 kN, suitable for medium-hard, regular-shape rocks.
   - **Small impact hammer** (backup): impact energy >=200 J/blow, suitable for irregular rocks difficult to align with press head.
   Both execution methods share one hydraulic loop, with autonomous selection by the robotic-arm control system based on rock shape and hardness.

**Decision logic**: end-effector stereo camera performs 3D scan of target rock. If diameter <=20 cm and shape is regular, use clamp directly. If diameter >20 cm, irregular, or embedded in bedrock, lower sealed hood and autonomously choose pressing or hammering. Crushed material remains in place as leveling fill for low-dust stripping operations.

**Key parameters**:

- **Mobility system**: small tracked chassis, power ~2 kW. Shares battery and autonomous navigation with rover fleet.
- **Hydraulic robotic arm**: 6 DOF, max reach 2 m, max end load >=500 kg (under lunar gravity). Quick-swap interface is hydraulically driven, switch time <=30 s. Power ~5 kW.
- **Battery capacity**: >=60 kWh, about 6-10 h operation per charge.
- **Operating temperature**: whole system adapted to -180C to 200C. PTFE seals; hydraulic fluid uses low-temperature silicone oil or NaK liquid metal.

**Deployment scheme**: deploy 2 units across mine site in Infrastructure Phase II, serving construction-aggregate and metal-mineral pits respectively. Each unit is paired with one small follower transport cart (payload >=2 t, battery >=50 kWh, endurance ~8 h) to receive clamped rocks and move them to centralized crushing points.

**(a) Impact crusher (primary scheme)**

- **Hammer material and hardness**: Hadfield steel (work-hardened >=55 HRC) or WC-Co carbide (>=86 HRA), see Section 4.5.2.
- **Unit power**: 10-15 kW (throughput 2-5 t/h), mass <=3,000 kg.
- **Dust and ejecta control**: fully sealed chamber + dual-layer airlock feed + screw-conveyor forced discharge + negative-pressure extraction; spring-energized PTFE seals.

**(b) Dual-layer vibratory screen (low-gravity optimized)**

### 4.14 Volume 4 Conclusion

This volume completes integrated planning for lunar industrial resource exploration and mining engineering, from systematic consolidation of mineral data at candidate sites and full-spectrum analysis of lunar extreme-environment effects on mining, to phased engineering plans for water-ice extraction, construction-aggregate mining, metal-mineral beneficiation, and helium-3/volatile extraction. It covers all raw-material needs for a permanent base to progress from "able to survive" to "able to manufacture."

**Core conclusions:**

1. **Water-ice mining must advance in phases; energy strategy determines extraction cost.** Stage 0 and Stage 1 rely on stand-alone electric heating (unit electricity <=50 kWh/kg), while Stage 2 switches to power-plant waste-heat recovery (target <=30 kWh/kg). This energy-strategy evolution is fully coupled to progress of power-system buildout in Volume 3.

2. **Completing coarse mineral processing at mine site is the superior engineering choice.** On-site crushing/screening can reduce transport mass by 30%-50%, and natural vacuum heat rejection on the surface is better than sealed underground base spaces. Fine screening and magnetic separation should remain at base for quality control and downstream process integration.

3. **Low-dust mining includes two alternative zero-dust routes plus one large-particle pretreatment device.** Microwave pre-sintering + mechanical stripping heats the regolith surface to >=1,100C for consolidation; contact-interface press-in/freeze/lift keeps the full process sealed with zero cutting and zero dust. For residual large particles, a self-propelled clamp-crush integrated unit combines clamping and sealed crushing and can autonomously choose the best mode in the field.

4. **Helium-3 and volatiles are formally incorporated as a fourth resource class into exploration and extraction planning.** Mare-basalt regions (especially Ti-rich Mare Tranquillitatis) are priority exploration targets, with proven-reserve target >=100 t. Extraction uses staged utilization process of "sealed heating-gas separation-tailings to bricks," achieving zero solid-waste discharge. The Interlune-Vermeer lunar excavator prototype and NASA Prospect Moon payload (launch in 2028) provide industrial-feasibility references [38][39].

5. **Ejecta from impact crushers under lunar vacuum/low gravity must be controlled by sealed design.** Fully sealed chamber + dual-layer airlock feed + screw-conveyor forced discharge + negative-pressure extraction; seals use spring-energized PTFE.

6. **Operating temperature ranges and hardness requirements for all key executive components are consolidated in Section 4.5.** Drilling, crushing, cutting, and sealing equipment are all designed for the harshest conditions (-250C to 200C).

7. **Energy, endurance, and launch mass for all mine-site equipment are fully accounted for.** Stage 1 total mine-site daily energy is ~1,036 kWh, and total equipment launch mass is ~19.8 t (net). Battery-powered equipment endurance is 6-10 hours. These data directly support transport-cost accounting in the Volume 10 economic model.

8. **Mineral-data scarcity remains the largest uncertainty.** Mineral data for Philolaus crater is close to zero and must be filled by Mission Groups 2 and 3 in Volume 1.
