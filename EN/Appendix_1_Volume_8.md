# # 8.3 Level VI warning and response system

### # 8.3.1 Multiple Risks and Response Principles

There may be multiple hazards at the base at the same time or in succession (e.g., the loss of pressure from the monthly shock-triggered module and a fire at the same time; an EVA accident during the SPE warning period). In such cases, the Emergency Control Centre (ECC) is guided by the following principles:

1. ** Top-level priority**: implementation of response at the highest level of risk of the risk involved when multiple risks are triggered simultaneously. For example, V-DQ (premonition of the monthly earthquake) and II-BS (depressure loss) responded to the full area evacuation at level II and, if both II-BS and I-AS (SPE) responded to full evacuation at level I.
2. **Unilateralization of Directives**: a uniform directive issued by the ECC with the highest level of warning numbers shall not be accompanied by conflicting orders of multiple levels. All types of risk identified must be listed in the directive for the overall situation to be understood by the various levels of response.
3. ** Subject compatibility**: The highest-level response action naturally covers the lower-level requirement (full evacuation of level I will necessarily include level II area evacuation and individual rescue requests of level III), but the ERT and the technical support team will be required to prioritize the lower-level risk that can be handled in parallel within the framework of the highest-level response (e.g., in the case of a third-CE person seeking help during the full I evacuation process, the ERT should still be out of the cabin for rescue, subject to return within the time limit for a class I asylum).
4. **Uniform Command of the ECC**: The authority to control all response capabilities at the time of the multi-hazard overlap is concentrated in the ECC. The response teams may not change their response levels or direction without the authorization of the ECC.

## # 8.3.3 Level I Base Catastrophe (I-AS:SPE) response process

Solar particle event (SPE) is the most challenging space environment emergency facing the Moon Base. Currently, SEP predicts that an empirical model, a physical model and a pattern of research that coexists with machine learning methods >100 MEV Proton Incident Alerts can be 30-60 minutes[1][2]. The following is the standard response process under the **I-AS** level alert:

** (a) Early warning reception phase (T-60-min to T-30-min)**

1. The circum-lunar space environment monitoring satellite (or the particle detector on board the Sun ' s high-energy particle fluxes) detected an automatic warning signal to the Base Control Centre. Existing SEP prediction models can provide 30-60-minute warning windows based on coronal observations and real-time particle fluxes [2].
The Control Centre Duty AI system verifies the origin of signals and particle flux data in <10 s, immediately triggers the entire base **I-AS** red alert (sonic alarm + full-band radio + individual terminal mandatory delivery) if they exceed the I level threshold.
3. The duty commander confirms the alarm and gives a full evacuation order in <60 s.

** (b) Personnel evacuation phase (T-30 Min to T-5 Min)**

1. All non-essential personnel immediately cease their current activities to the nearest storm shelter along the designated evacuation route (see section 8.4.1). This factor has been factored into the estimation of the time of evacuation routes at lower-gravity step speeds compared to the slower pace of the Earth and lower step frequency.
2. EVA personnel immediately suspended their operations and returned to the nearest airlock as quickly as possible. If EVA personnel on the patrol were unable to return to the base in 15 min to perform the “shelter-in-place” procedure, the closure of the patrol unit's non-essential power-consuming equipment was to wait for rescue by wearing the emergency radiation protection vest as a temporary shield (Equivalent shield 5 g/cm2, wearing low gravity without affecting basic mobility). Radiometric data from the Artemis I mission also confirmed the effectiveness of such protection strategies [8].
3. The Control Centre tracks the withdrawal of all personnel through individual terminals in real time.
4. All persons must have completed their refuge before the SSE high-energy particle front arrives.

** (c) Asylum maintenance phase (SPE duration usually 1-3 days)**

1. Storm Shelter is independent ECLSS (30 days self-held).
2. Persons in the shelter wear a personal radiation dosage to continuously monitor cumulative doses. All loose items in the shelter are fixed by magic adhesion or flexible beams to adapt to the movement of items under low gravity.
3. The Control Centre keeps in contact with all persons through the communication terminals in the shelter and regularly informs the SPE traffic trends.
The medical station (section 7.7.1) enters the emergency mode in preparation for possible over-exposure.

** (d) Recovery and assessment phase**

1. The circum-lunar space environment monitoring satellite confirms that the flux of high-energy particles has dropped below the threshold of level I above 2 h.
The Control Centre instructs persons to return to their jobs in an orderly manner.
3. Radiation dose verification of all personnel. Prioritized rotation of people over 50 mSv per year to return to Earth.

### # 8.3.4 Type II serious incident (II-BS: seal failure/dispressure) response process

** (a) Detection and early warning**

- All pressurized sections are equipped with an online pressure sensor (precision 0.1 kPa) and ultrasound leak locator (sensitivity 1 cm 3/s@70 kPa pressure variance).
- Control Centre automatically triggers **IV-BS** orange warning when pressure drops > 0.5 kPa/min in any class; triggers **II-BS** red warning when drops > 5 kPa/min.

** (b) Response process**

1. ** Auto-separation**: Air-classified isolation doors at both ends of the depressure segment are automatically closed at < 5 s (spring + electromagnetic triggers do not depend on external power sources) after a decrease in pressure has been detected. The separation door has been designed to take into account the impact of low-gravity self-heavy reduction on closing speed using spring-assisted acceleration of closure). At this point, the disaster separation system (see section 8.4.4) becomes self-executing.
2. ** Evacuation of personnel**: personnel in the cabin were immediately in close proximity to an emergency oxygen mask (two under each section). Flex-weaving, with flexible woven webs, does not drift over the face at low gravity) and evacuates along the evacuation route to the adjacent safe-class section or to a nearby level II emergency shelter.
3. **Possibility and leakage**: ERT fast-tracks the damage points according to the ultrasound leak locator. The initial containment was completed within <30 min using the emergency containment package (SPUA speed-jet sealant + CFRP supplement + inflatable temporary airlock). The SPUA Sealing Guidelines reduce the optimal contact time to 15 s for movement characteristics dominated by low-gravity surface tension.
4. **Repressure**: after repair, the adjacent section will gradually restore the isolation section to 70 kPa repressure rate ≤1 kPa/min through the emergency repressure tube.

** (c) Minimum living conditions**

In the worst cases (e.g., when the multiple sections were not under pressure and could not be repaired immediately) all personnel were evacuated to the storm shelter at level I.

### # 8.3.5 Type II serious incident (II-BH: fire) response process

** (a) Preventive measures**

- Disaster separation systems (see section 8.4.4) are the basis for fire prevention and control. Firewalls and firewalls were integrated during the construction phase. Fires between adjacent sections designed for a single fire section using a stand-alone fire protection section are not designed to spread to the adjacent section.
- Multi-spectral flame detectors (UV+ IR-band response ≤50 ms) and inhaled smoke detectors (0.01% obs/m) in all pressurized sections. (c) An appropriate encryption space for low gravity detectors [6][7].
- The use of open fire in the pressurized chamber is strictly prohibited. All heating equipment shall be equipped with an overheated automatic power outage.
- All-base fabric passes the UL 94 V-0 flame retardation test [13].

** (b) Response process**

1. ** Detection and alert**: The Control Centre launched **II-BH** Red alarm within < 1 s after the flame or smoke detector triggers the non-essential power supply of the fire module (retention of lighting and communication). The fire doors, which are also adjacent to the fire-protected area, are automatically closed. The ESA study indicated that a smoke-integrated detector within the back wind pipe could reduce the delay in low-gravity low fire detection [7].
2. ** Fire suppression**: ERT arrived at the scene within < 3 min. Fire extinguisher - Electrical fire extinguishers use CO2 fire extinguishers or fine water mist fire extinguishers (water mist particles <50 μm do not conduct electricity). The vent design ensures effective cover of burning surfaces for low-gravity droplets; the use of dry powder extinguishers and halon fire extinguishers is strictly prohibited. Overview of spacecraft fire suppression techniques for fire-fighting strategies [6].
**Frozen gas control**: ventilated in the fire module to "smoking mode" - – Reduction of wind delivery to 20 per cent return to 100 per cent flue gas filtered through the Hepa+ activated carbon into an outdoor vacuum. The setting of fire protection valves inside the pipe automatically closes when the temperature within the pipe is detected > 150°C to prevent the fire from spreading through the ventilation pipe to other fire protection subdivisions.
4.** Evacuation of personnel**: evacuation of all personnel in affected areas to a near-level II emergency shelter when the fire could not be controlled within 5min. Firefighters wear self-sufficient respirators (30 centimetres of oxygen). The mask is sealed on the face under low gravity by a flexible head-net). If the fire spreads to multiple sections to upgrade to ** Level I** (base level disaster).

** (c) Controlled exhausts (opt-out means)**

Controlled exhausts for fire compartments may be ordered by ECC if all of the following conditions are met:

All personnel in the fire module have been identified as evacuated or as non-viable by means of a life detector.
2. The air-container doors in the adjacent section have been closed and the pressure stabilized (indicating that the isolation is effective).
Fires cannot be controlled within 30 min by conventional fire suppression.
4. Ventilation does not result in toxic flue gas being poured into other compartments through a public ventilation pipeline (smoke pipe fire valves have been closed).

Operational step: ECC remotely opens the exterior exhaust valve in the fire module at a controlled rate (pressure-down rate of 10 kPa/min) and discharges the atmosphere into the moon surface vacuum. The combustion ends immediately when oxygen is drained. A vacuum of 15 min is maintained at the end of the vent (recognizing that all remaining ashes are completely extinguished). Repressure shall be performed after ERT has confirmed that there is no risk of re-burning in the module. The module that was used to perform controlled exhaust suppression must be thoroughly inspected by ERT for structural integrity prior to repressure.

**Conditions**: Controlled exhaust fires do not apply in cases where there are still survivors in the module; vent failure or piping is blocked by debris; quarantine integrity of adjacent fire-protected areas is not confirmed; fire has ignited chamber structure material (structure integrity may have been damaged vent may have caused uncontrolled structural breakdown).

Note: The NASA SAFFIRE series of in-orbit experiments have completed several large-scale fire experiments on the Cygnus spacecraft to validate microgravity material flammability and flame transmission models [14].

## 8.4.4 Disaster segregation system

Disaster segregation is the central defence against the spread of sector-level incidents such as fire, toxic spills and stress loss to base-level disasters. The system consists of three layers:

** (a) First floor: Active isolation - air isolation door**

- All the routes between the pressurized sections of the module are closed to the air ( spring + electromagnetic trigger does not depend on external power). The isolation doors are automatically closed under the following conditions: >0.5 kPa/min (pressure loss separation) was detected; the flame or smoke detector triggers the boundary door in the fire-proof zone where the source is located (fire quarantine); and the toxic gas sensor triggers (spill isolation).
- Both sides of the pressure differential after the closure of the isolation door are designed to withstand 100 kPa (i.e., the constant pressure on the full vacuum side of one side) to ensure that the pressure loss does not spread.
- The closing signal of the isolation door was simultaneously sent to ECC and highlighted on the ground control screen the physical boundaries of the affected area.

** (b) Second floor: passive isolation - firewalls and firewalls**

- Fireproof structures are used for the wall between the adjacent fire partitions: 50 mm thick aerogel insulation in the middle of the double-layer aluminium alloy panels (3 mm thick). Fire-resistant thallium 120 min (with a low-gravity fire behaviour different from that of the insulation of gravity according to the temperature curve at ISO 834-1).
- Installation of fire-proof valves (automatic closure at >150°C at > 150°C) along the ventilation pipeline to prevent the spread of the fire through the pipeline.
- The addition of separate fire-segregation segments between industrial areas (high-temperature workshops such as metal smelting and CNT manufacturing) and residential areas (air-segregated doors at each end of the non-pressure transition area of 5 m length). The design ensures that even full-class fires in the industrial zone do not result in smoke intrusion into the settlement.

** (c) Third floor: Active isolation - negative pressure gradient isolation**

- If the release of toxic substances (IV-BH) cannot be immediately blocked by the ECC in the section where the source is located, the negative pressure gradient between the affected section and the adjacent safe segment can be established through the ventilation system (the amount of wind return from the affected segment is up to 120% to 30% to maintain Zen10 Pa). This measure ensures that any trace leakage can only flow from the safe segment to the affected section rather than to the reverse diffusion.
- The negative pressure isolation in the medical ward isolation ward (section 7.7.1) is maintained on the same basis and 15 Pa constant negative pressure.

** (d) Certification criteria for quarantine systems**

- Firewall maximum fire tolerance 120 min.
- Cyclical isolation door from trigger to complete closure of 5 s.
- Fire valves melt off 10 s.
- Negative pressure gradients to build 60 s.

# # 8.11 Conclusion of volume VIII

The volume completes the systematic planning of the lunar base emergency protection and rescue system and transforms the survival philosophy of volume XII of the main book, "Close of civilization" into an enforceable engineering programme and operating process. Core contributions are:

** A two-tiered system of classification codes and level VI risk assessments has been established and the principle of the highest level of response has been established for multiple risk overlaps. ** The classification codes for four major categories A/B/C/D plus internal numbers are seamlessly aligned with the level VI risk level through the “level-class number” alert format. Discrepancies are avoided when multiple risks are triggered by a uniform response order at the highest level involved.
2. **Standard operating processes for the six-stage early warning are fully covered and the system adapts to low gravity environments. ** The complete process is given as a typical example of I-AS (SPE full evacuation), II-BS (sealed zone evacuation), II-BH (fire zone evacuation with options for vacuum suppression), III-CE (EVA personal rescue). All facilities, equipment, materials and operations in the volume are all marked with low-gravity impact and adaptation measures ranging from the optimization of fire extinguisher vents to an estimate of the impact time from portable rescue kits to evacuation time.
** A three-layer disaster separation system has been established. ** The air-diffusion doors (active isolation < 5 s off), firewalls/fire-proofing plates (passive isolation < 120 min fire-resistant maximum pipe-proof valves), and negative-pressure gradients (active isolation < 60 s established) constitute three lines of defence against fire, leak and loss of pressure.
4. ** The three-tier emergency shelter system was planned and a multi-hazard asylum strategy identified. ** Category I storm shelter (Zen 30 days SPE decline rate of 99.9 per cent), Level II multipurpose emergency shelter (Zen 7 days), Level III area protection parameters are based on large SPE historical event data.
5. ** Updated results of research on space security have been introduced. ** Consolidation of authoritative literature such as the SPE Forecast Model Overview, Melvin Tube Collapse Probability Assessment, Low Gravity Fire Behaviour Study, NASA SAFFFIRE On-orbit Fire Experiment.
6. ** Normalization mechanisms for training and exercises have been established. ** Full staff 72 h emergency training (including low-gravity physical exercise), quarterly evacuation exercises, monthly ERT special exercises, annual black-start exercises, semi-annual multi-hazard superimplementing desktops.


##8.12 Link to volume 12 of the main book

This volume applies specifically to the Moon Base scene, as presented in volume XII of the main volume, the “3 Layers of Disaster Prevention System” (passive survival design — active emergency response — rapid recovery). Of which:

- The concept of the “firewall” and “fortress of life” in the disaster separation system (a high-intensity door + firewall + negative pressure gradient) and the physical redundancy design of the shelter corresponding to the home book;
- Six-degree early warning (the highest-level response principle, with multiple risk overlaps) and the “active emergency response” of the ERT rapid response home book;
- Lifeline Project, which corresponds to the main handbook of disaster recovery priorities and long-term reconstruction strategies.

The core difference between the Moon Base and the Space Circle is that the Moon Base is gravitational, ground-based and has local resources that are more resilient at the disaster recovery level, and that the Base also has a unique fire-fighting tool to take advantage of the moon-surface vacuum, which is not available at the Base. However, the Base was unable to limit the extent of the loss by mechanical breakpoints, as was the case with the ring ladder. The present volume therefore places particular emphasis on the synergy between the three-layer disaster separation system and storm shelter I as a “base-in-base” fully independent survival unit, which forms the bottom line of the Moon Base.
