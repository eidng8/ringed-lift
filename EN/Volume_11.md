## 11.1 Preface

This volume is positioned as the **legal applicability analysis and international governance framework design** for the Space Ring Elevator project, focusing on answering the following core questions:

> The Space Ring Elevator, a closed-loop infrastructure spanning six continents along the equator and permanently occupying a 100 km orbit at the outer edge of GEO—who owns it? Who governs it? How is legal applicability defined during construction and operation? How do investing countries share costs and profits? How is liability allocated in the event of an accident?

This volume follows:
- **Overview**: Project vision and operational principles (UN unified operation, principal and interest settlement according to investment ratio)
- **Volume X**: Total investment and financing structure (tens of trillions, diversified mix of sovereign funds/multilateral banks/private capital)
- **Volume III**: Specific site selection for 6 ground nodes (involving the territories of Brazil, Gabon, Ecuador, Kiribati, Indonesia, and the high seas of the Indian Ocean)
- **Volumes IV, V, VI**: Technical parameters of the ring body, elevator cable, and cabins

Core tasks of this volume:

1. Sort out all international legal frameworks involved in the Space Ring Elevator, identify the applicability, gaps, and potential conflicts of existing laws to this project.
2. Draw on precedents such as the International Space Station (ISS), International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor (ITER), and International Seabed Authority (ISA) to design the international governance framework for the Space Ring Elevator.
3. Draft core articles of the UN operational model charter.
4. Establish mechanisms for investment recognition, principal and interest settlement, and exit for investing countries.
5. Construct a complete legal liability chain for debris removal, accident investigation, damage compensation, and dispute resolution.
6. Clarify intellectual property ownership, commercial access licensing, and tax rules.


## 11.2 Analysis of International Law Applicability

### 11.2.1 Involved International Legal Frameworks

The physical form of the Space Ring Elevator—extending from the ground to the outer edge of GEO, crossing the territories of six sovereign states and the high seas, and permanently occupying specific orbital resources—subjects it to multiple constraints from **space law**, **maritime law**, **aviation law**, **international telecommunications law**, and **international environmental law**.

| International Treaty/Framework | Core Content | Applicability to Space Ring Elevator |
|-------------------------------|--------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Outer Space Treaty (1967)** | Outer space is not subject to national appropriation; states bear international responsibility for their activities in outer space | **Applicable**. The GEO segment of the ring and elevator cable is in outer space; no state may claim sovereignty |
| **Moon Agreement (1979)** | The Moon and its natural resources are the common heritage of mankind | **Partially applicable**. If lunar CNT manufacturing involves lunar resource extraction, the non-appropriation principle must be observed |
| **International Telecommunication Union (ITU) Framework** | Allocation of GEO orbital positions and spectrum | **Highly relevant**. The ring occupies a 100 km annular region at the outer edge of GEO; while it does not directly occupy GEO communication satellite slots, it may cause passive intermodulation interference (PIM) to adjacent frequencies |
| **United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (1982)** | Division of rights over territorial seas, exclusive economic zones, and the high seas | **Applicable**. Elevator cables cross the EEZs of coastal states; the Indian Ocean platform is located on the high seas |
| **Convention on International Civil Aviation (Chicago Convention, 1944)** | States have sovereignty over the airspace above their territory | **Partially applicable**. From the ground up to about 100 km is national airspace; operation of elevator cables and low-altitude cabins requires anchor state approval |
| **Space Debris Mitigation Guidelines (IADC/UNCOPUOS)** | Prevention and removal of space debris | **Applicable**. The ring elevator must undertake its own debris removal obligations and avoid generating new debris |
| **Declaration of Legal Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space (1963)** | Outer space activities should benefit all countries | **Applicable**. Operation of the ring elevator must embody the "benefit of all mankind" principle |

### 11.2.2 Three-Tiered Jurisdiction

The physical structure of the Space Ring Elevator spans three legal spaces, each subject to different jurisdictional rules:

| Segment | Altitude Range | Legal Space | Jurisdiction |
|---------|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Ground Anchor Segment** | 0–approx. 100 m | National territory | Exclusive jurisdiction of anchor states (Brazil, Gabon, Ecuador, Kiribati, Indonesia) |
| **Atmospheric Segment (Low Altitude)** | 100 m–approx. 100 km | National airspace | Anchor states have jurisdiction over cable segments passing through their airspace; Indian Ocean platform is on the high seas, not involving coastal state airspace |
| **Outer Space Segment (Mid/High Altitude to GEO)** | >100 km | Outer space (global commons) | Not under any national jurisdiction; unified jurisdiction by the international management body of the ring elevator |

### 11.2.3 Key Gaps in Existing Law and Responses

The current international space law system, established in the 1960s–70s, did not anticipate "permanently Earth-attached closed-loop space infrastructure" like the Space Ring Elevator. The following key legal gaps must be filled by new international agreements:

| Legal Gap | Physical Scenario | Response |
|-----------|------------------|----------|
| **Orbital Occupancy Rights** | The ring permanently occupies a 100 km annular region at the outer edge of GEO, potentially affecting GEO communication satellites in adjacent orbits | Coordinate with ITU to designate the ring's annular region as a "special space infrastructure protection zone," coexisting with GEO satellites |
| **Right of Transit** | Elevator cables extend vertically from anchor state territory, passing through the airspace above coastal states' EEZs but not touching other states' airspace | Sign "innocent passage agreements" with relevant coastal states, clarifying that cables do not constitute infringement or resource claims over the EEZ |
| **Jurisdiction over High Seas Platform** | The floating platform in the Indian Ocean is on the high seas, legally similar to ships or artificial islands | Refer to UNCLOS provisions on artificial islands on the high seas; register and exercise exclusive jurisdiction by the international management body |
| **Collective Management of GEO Resources** | Operation of the ring may cause electromagnetic interference or physical proximity to GEO communication satellites | Establish ring-satellite coexistence agreements, set minimum safety distances and frequency coordination mechanisms |
| **Legal Status of Counterweight Asteroids** | Counterweights are captured C-type asteroids, transformed from natural celestial bodies into part of a "man-made" facility | Refer to Article 8 of the Outer Space Treaty: the registering state retains jurisdiction over space objects. Counterweights should be registered as part of the ring elevator |

### 11.2.4 Analogy with the International Seabed Authority (ISA)

The International Seabed Authority (ISA), established to manage the "common heritage of mankind" (mineral resources in the international seabed area), provides a meaningful governance precedent. The GEO outer edge orbit occupied by the Space Ring Elevator is also a "global commons," and a portion of its operating income can be included in an **international public fund** to support developing countries' participation in space science and education.


## 11.3 International Governance Framework: United Nations Organization for the Ring Elevator (UNORE)

### 11.3.1 Framework Design Principles

International governance of the Space Ring Elevator must adhere to the following principles:
- **Benefit of All Mankind**: After investment costs are recovered, operating income should be used for global public purposes (scientific research, education, disaster reduction, etc.)
- **Matching Investment and Decision-Making Power**: Investing countries have decision-making weight proportional to their investment, but the technical executive body holds veto power over operational safety and technical standards
- **Open Participation**: Non-investing countries may participate and receive corresponding benefits by providing technology, personnel, or resources (such as lunar manufacturing materials)
- **Peaceful Use**: The ring elevator strictly prohibits any military use, including weapons deployment, military transport, and intelligence gathering

### 11.3.2 Organizational Structure

Drawing on the multilateral governance models of ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) and the International Space Station (ISS), it is recommended to establish the **United Nations Organization for the Ring Elevator (UNORE)** as a specialized agency under the United Nations.

| Body | Function | Composition |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Council** | Highest decision-making body, decides budget, admits new members, approves major technical changes | Representatives of investing countries, voting weight allocated according to investment ratio |
| **Technical Executive Committee** | Formulation and revision of technical standards, safety review, accident investigation | Representatives of each member state's space agency (CNSA, NASA, ESA, JAXA, etc.), one vote per country |
| **Operations Management Bureau** | Daily operations, maintenance scheduling, transport service contracts, electricity and communications sales | Professional operations team, publicly recruited by the Council |
| **Finance and Audit Committee** | Investment recognition, principal and interest settlement, annual audit | Representatives of investing countries' finance ministries + independent audit institutions |
| **Legal and Dispute Resolution Committee** | Interpretation of legal applicability, contract dispute arbitration, damage compensation rulings | International law experts, legal representatives of investing countries |
| **Science and Education Committee** | Scientific project review, educational outreach, developing country participation programs | Representatives from national academies and universities |

### 11.3.3 Dual Role of Anchor States and Investing Countries

The six elevator anchor states have a special status:
- As **sovereign states**, they have territorial jurisdiction over ground anchor facilities and cable segments within their airspace
- As **investing countries**, they have decision-making weight in the Council according to their investment ratio
- Regardless of investment amount, anchor states have veto power over the safe operation of elevator cable segments passing through their territory/airspace

The relationship between anchor states and the international management body of the ring elevator is defined by **bilateral site agreements**—similar to the agreements between CERN (European Organization for Nuclear Research) and its host countries, France and Switzerland.


## 11.4 Investment Recognition and Principal-Interest Settlement Scheme

### 11.4.1 Recognition of Investment Forms

Investment by participating countries is not limited to cash and may include the following forms, converted into "investment units" at the agreed exchange rate or fair value:

| Investment Form | Example | Recognition Method |
|-----------------|---------|-------------------|
| Cash Investment | Direct allocation of construction funds | Recognized by actual payment amount |
| Technology Contribution | Provision of heavy rocket launch services, CNT manufacturing technology | Assessed at fair market value |
| In-Kind Contribution | Provision of launch sites, ports, anchor land | Recognized at appraised value |
| Personnel Secondment | Dispatch of engineers, scientists for construction | Converted according to international salary standards |
| Resource Contribution | Lunar manufacturing materials, asteroid counterweights | Assessed at fair market value |

### 11.4.2 Principal-Interest Settlement Scheme

According to the operational principles in the overview: **Before construction costs are fully recovered, the United Nations will periodically settle principal and interest with all investing countries according to their actual investment ratio.**

| Parameter | Suggested Value | Description |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Interest Rate Benchmark | SDR rate + 1.5%/year | SDR (Special Drawing Rights) rate approx. 2%–3%, plus 1.5% equals approx. 3.5%–4.5% |
| Settlement Cycle | Every 5 years | Synchronized with project evaluation cycle |
| Debt Repayment Source | 70% of net operating income from the ring elevator | Transport services, solar power, communications, and tourism income |
| Early Repayment | Allowed | No penalty |
| Default Handling | If in arrears for 3 years, voting weight is frozen in proportion to arrears | Protects the rights of other investing countries |

### 11.4.3 Exit and Transfer Mechanism

- **Exit**: An investing country may exit the project with 5 years' advance notice and after settling all outstanding debts. Its equity will be purchased at fair market value by the remaining investing countries in proportion.
- **Transfer**: An investing country may transfer part or all of its equity to other sovereign states or multilateral institutions, subject to Council approval. Private capital may participate indirectly through sovereign funds.


## 11.5 Safety and Liability Mechanisms

### 11.5.1 Allocation of Debris Removal Obligations

As a giant space infrastructure, the Space Ring Elevator is both a potential victim of space debris (impacts) and a potential generator (micro-debris from cabin operation, construction).

| Scenario | Responsible Party | Removal Obligation |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|
| Debris generated by the ring elevator itself | Operations Management Bureau | Must be removed to the lowest level achievable by current technology |
| Damage caused by third-party debris impact | Debris source country (if identifiable) or Operations Management Bureau | Operations Management Bureau repairs first, then Legal Committee seeks compensation from responsible party |
| Debris impact with untraceable source | Operations Management Bureau (public liability) | Funded from the ring elevator public maintenance fund |

### 11.5.2 Accident Investigation and Compensation Mechanism

Accidents involving the Space Ring Elevator may involve multiple jurisdictions—occurring in outer space (global commons), but with consequences potentially affecting nationals or property of anchor states.

**Accident Classification**:

| Level | Description | Investigating Body |
|:-----:|------------|-------------------|
| I | Minor malfunction (equipment damage, no casualties) | Internal investigation by Operations Management Bureau |
| II | Serious malfunction (casualties or major property loss) | Special investigation team of Technical Executive Committee |
| III | Catastrophic accident (ring disintegration, elevator breakage) | Independent investigation committee appointed by the UN Secretary-General |

**Compensation Liability**: Refer to Articles VI and VII of the Outer Space Treaty—states bear international responsibility for their activities in outer space (including those of non-governmental entities). The ring elevator, as a whole, is civilly liable through the Operations Management Bureau. The compensation fund of the Operations Management Bureau is reserved at 5% of annual operating income.

### 11.5.3 Dispute Resolution Mechanism

**Tiered Dispute Resolution**:

1. **Negotiation**: Parties seek resolution through direct negotiation within 60 days
2. **Mediation**: Mediator appointed by the Legal and Dispute Resolution Committee, mediation period of 90 days
3. **Arbitration**: Arbitration tribunal of 3 arbitrators (each party appoints 1, the third appointed by the Permanent Court of Arbitration), award is final
4. **International Court**: Disputes involving treaty interpretation may be submitted to the International Court of Justice


## 11.6 Intellectual Property and Commercial Access

### 11.6.1 Intellectual Property of In-Orbit Scientific Achievements

Microgravity laboratories, space telescopes, and gravitational wave detectors on the ring will generate a large number of scientific results and patents. Intellectual property ownership follows **rules analogous to the International Space Station**:

- **Territorial Principle**: Inventions completed in modules registered to a particular country on the ring belong to that country
- **Sharing Principle**: Results from multinational cooperative experiments are shared according to the proportions agreed in the cooperation agreement
- **Disclosure Principle**: Results from pure basic scientific research conducted on public experimental platforms (directly managed by the Operations Management Bureau) should be published within a reasonable period

### 11.6.2 Commercial Access and Licensing

| Type of Commercial Activity | Licensing Conditions | Taxation |
|----------------------------|---------------------|----------|
| Transport Services | Service contract with Operations Management Bureau | Operations Management Bureau collects unified transport fees, profits shared among investing countries according to investment ratio |
| In-Orbit Manufacturing | Requires approval from Operations Management Bureau, compliance with safety standards | 5% facility usage fee based on ground market value of finished products |
| Tourism Operations | Must meet safety standards, passenger cabins and routes physically separated from cargo transport | 10% franchise fee on tourism income |
| Communications/Energy Services | Long-term purchase agreement with Operations Management Bureau | Operations Management Bureau collects unified fees, profits shared among investing countries according to investment ratio |


## 11.7 Draft Charter for UN Operational Model (Core Articles)

**Article 1: Organization Name and Legal Status**
The organization shall be named the United Nations Organization for the Ring Elevator (UNORE), a specialized agency under the United Nations, enjoying international legal personality.

**Article 2: Purpose**
(a) To construct, maintain, and operate the Space Ring Elevator, ensuring it serves the peaceful purposes of all mankind;
(b) To reduce orbital access costs and promote participation of developing countries in space activities;
(c) To promote global sharing of space science, technology, and clean energy.

**Article 3: Membership**
All UN member states may apply to join. Membership of investing countries is consistent with their investment obligations. Non-investing countries may apply for observer status, with the right to attend Council meetings.

**Article 4: Decision-Making Mechanism**
(a) 70% of Council voting weight is allocated according to cumulative investment ratio, 30% is one-country-one-vote;
(b) Decisions involving the territorial security of anchor states require anchor state consent;
(c) Changes to technical and safety standards require a two-thirds majority in the Technical Executive Committee.

**Article 5: Distribution of Operating Income**
Before construction costs (principal + interest) are fully recovered, 100% of net operating income is used for principal and interest repayment. After recovery, 50% of net income is used for maintenance and upgrades, 30% goes to the international public fund (for global public welfare), and 20% is distributed to investing countries according to investment ratio.

**Article 6: Exit Clause**
(See Section 10.4.3)

**Article 7: Amendment and Termination**
Amendments to this charter require a three-quarters majority in the Council. Termination of the organization requires approval by the UN General Assembly, with remaining assets distributed to then-members according to investment ratio.

## 11.9 Conclusion of Volume XI

This volume has sorted out the complex international legal frameworks involved in the Space Ring Elevator project and proposed a multilateral governance structure centered on the **United Nations Organization for the Ring Elevator (UNORE)**. Key conclusions:

1. **Three-tiered jurisdiction for the ring elevator**: Anchor states have territorial jurisdiction over ground and airspace segments, while the outer space segment is under unified UNORE jurisdiction. There are key gaps in current space law (orbital occupancy rights, right of transit, high seas platform status) that must be filled by new international agreements.
2. **Governance structure draws on the successful experiences of ITER, ISS, and ISA**: A three-tier structure of Council (investment-weighted voting), Technical Executive Committee (one-country-one-vote), and professional Operations Management Bureau balances investment fairness and technical safety.
3. **Principal-interest settlement scheme based on the operational principles in the overview**: SDR+1.5% interest rate, 5-year settlement cycle, after recovery, 50% of net income for maintenance, 30% to international public fund, 20% to investing countries.
4. **Safety and liability mechanisms cover the full chain of debris removal, accident investigation, and dispute resolution**, ensuring clear legal basis for the rights and obligations of all countries over the centuries-long operational period.
5. **The "benefit of all mankind" principle of the ring elevator is reflected not only in the distribution of operating income, but also in providing all countries (regardless of investment) with low-cost orbital access, seamless global communications, and clean energy.**
