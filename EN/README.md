## Literature and Background Review: The Evolution from "Orbital Ring" to "Space Ringed-Elevation"

### 1. Early Concepts: Theoretical Foundations of the Space Elevator

The basic concept of connecting Earth and space with a cable dates back to the late 19th century:

- **Konstantin Tsiolkovsky** proposed in 1895 the construction of a "ground-based tower" extending from the surface directly to geostationary orbit. Although limited by the material strength of the time, he soon realized the concept was not feasible then, but this laid the foundation for all subsequent tensile space elevator research [1].
- **Yuri Artsutanov** formally proposed the "tensile cable" structure in 1960: instead of building a tower to bear compressive stress, a cable would hang down from beyond geostationary orbit, tensioned by the centrifugal force of Earth's rotation. This theoretical model remains the starting point for all modern space elevator proposals [2].

### 2. The Proposal and Evolution of the "Orbital Ring" Concept

Almost concurrent with the space elevator, the "orbital ring" concept has also undergone multiple evolutions. This concept emphasizes constructing a **closed-loop structure** rather than a single-point cable:

- **Nikola Tesla**, in his later years (circa 1930s), envisioned a giant metal ring encircling Earth's equator in his research notes. However, detailed engineering analysis was lacking.
- **Paul Birch** formally published the "orbital ring" system in 1982 in the Journal of the British Interplanetary Society. He systematically proposed building a giant, rapidly rotating ring in low Earth orbit, with 20 or more cables suspended to the ground, forming a global transportation network. This proposal covered physical stability, material and cost estimates, and is a **milestone document** in orbital ring engineering [3].
- **Arthur C. Clarke**, in his science fiction works "The Fountains of Paradise" and "3001: The Final Odyssey," also envisioned a "world ring" at geostationary orbit altitude.

### 3. Key Material Breakthrough: Discovery and Application of Carbon Nanotubes

The core bottleneck constraining all previous proposals was materials. In 1991, **Sumio Iijima** discovered carbon nanotubes, whose theoretical tensile strength is estimated at 200 GPa, with extremely low density (about 1.3 g/cm³). This brought hope at the material level for constructing cables tens of thousands of kilometers long for the first time [7]. Since then, all modern engineering designs related to space elevators or orbital rings have regarded carbon nanotubes as the preferred or only feasible structural material.

### 4. Recent Related Proposals and Engineering Progress

Major countries and regions have successively launched research programs on space elevators or related supporting technologies, with typical examples including:

- **U.S. Space Elevator Competitions and Edwards Proposal**: In the early 2000s, Edwards and other scholars, relying on NASA's Institute for Advanced Concepts, proposed a more mature "ribbon" design for the space elevator, advocating the use of ground-based lasers to power climbers, and conducted staged ground competitions [4].
- **Japan Obayashi Corporation Proposal**: In 2012, Japanese construction company Obayashi Corporation proposed the ambitious "Space Elevator 2050" plan, intending to use carbon nanotube materials to build a cable 96,000 kilometers long, with a cabin speed of 200 km/h taking about 7 days for a one-way trip to geostationary orbit [5].
