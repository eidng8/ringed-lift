## Literature and Background Review: The Evolution from "Orbital Ring" to "Space Ring Elevator"

### 1. Early Concepts: Theoretical Foundations of the Space Elevator

The core idea of connecting Earth and space with a cable can be traced to the late 19th century:

- **Konstantin Tsiolkovsky** proposed in 1895 a "ground-based tower" extending from Earth to geostationary orbit. Although constrained by the material strength of his era, and soon recognized as infeasible at the time, this idea laid the foundation for all later tensile space elevator research [1].
- **Yuri Artsutanov** formally proposed a "tensile cable" architecture in 1960: instead of building a compression-bearing tower, a cable would hang down from beyond geostationary orbit and be tensioned by centrifugal force from Earth rotation. This model remains the starting point of modern space elevator concepts [2].

### 2. The Proposal and Evolution of the "Orbital Ring" Concept

Almost in parallel with the space elevator, the orbital ring concept also evolved through multiple stages. It emphasizes a **closed-loop structure** rather than a single cable point:

- **Nikola Tesla**, in his later years (around the 1930s), had already imagined a giant metallic ring around Earth's equator in his notes, though without detailed engineering proof.
- **Paul Birch** formally published the orbital ring system in 1982 in the *Journal of the British Interplanetary Society*. He systematically proposed a rapidly rotating giant ring in near-Earth orbit, with 20 or more tethers suspended to the ground to form a global transportation network. The work addressed physical stability, material requirements, and cost estimates, and is a **milestone publication** for orbital ring engineering [3].
- **Arthur C. Clarke**, in *The Fountains of Paradise* and *3001: The Final Odyssey*, also envisioned a "world ring" at geostationary altitude.

### 3. Key Materials Breakthrough: Discovery and Application of Carbon Nanotubes

The core bottleneck in all earlier concepts was materials. In 1991, **Sumio Iijima** discovered carbon nanotubes, with theoretical tensile strength estimates up to 200 GPa and very low density (about 1.3 g/cm^3). This was the first serious materials-level hope for cables tens of thousands of kilometers long [7]. Since then, modern designs for space elevators and orbital rings have treated carbon nanotubes as the preferred, or only feasible, structural material.

### 4. Recent Related Concepts and Engineering Progress

Major countries and regions have launched studies on space elevators and related enabling technologies. Representative examples include:

- **U.S. space elevator competitions and the Edwards concept**: In the early 2000s, Edwards and others, through the NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts, proposed a more mature ribbon-style elevator design, advocated ground-based laser power for climbers, and organized staged ground competitions [4].
- **Obayashi proposal (Japan)**: In 2012, Japanese construction firm Obayashi proposed the ambitious "Space Elevator 2050" concept, planning a 96,000 km cable based on carbon nanotubes. A 200 km/h cabin would require about 7 days one-way to geostationary orbit [5].
- **China Chang'e program and new-material findings**: In 2026, a **Jilin University team** reported natural single-walled carbon nanotubes in Chang'e-6 lunar soil samples, providing indirect support for lunar in-situ manufacturing [6].
- **In-situ resource utilization and asteroid mining**: Concepts like lunar-soil 3D printing, space spider robots, and asteroid capture are frontier directions aimed at lowering transport cost and enabling initial extraterrestrial self-sufficiency. These lines of thought are integrated into the space ring elevator proposal.

### 5. Summary and Positioning

At the conceptual level, this proposal **inherits** Paul Birch's orbital-ring closed-loop network and Artsutanov's tensile space-elevator principles. On materials, it **continues** the carbon nanotube trajectory established since Iijima. The contribution of this work is:

1. Combining these concepts into a unified ring elevator system located at the **outer edge of geostationary orbit**.
2. Using the full resource logic of "**in-situ CNT manufacturing from lunar regolith**" plus "**capture of carbon sources from C-type asteroids**" as a core engineering premise, pushing the proposal from concept toward a feasibility argument.
3. Providing staged **conservative engineering parameters, node siting, investment scale, and a multi-physics mechanics framework**, forming a complete engineering blueprint supported by cross-domain evidence from physics and materials engineering.

In principle, this proposal **does not claim original ownership of the fundamental concepts of the orbital ring or space elevator**. It is a comprehensive engineering argument built upon over a century of prior exploration.

## V. Core Findings

1. **A standalone single space elevator has very poor economics**. Construction cost is roughly 10-15 trillion CNY while annual throughput is only 0.57 (10k tons). Regardless of lifespan, unit cost stays around 17 to 53 million CNY per ton, about **3-4 orders of magnitude** above reusable rockets. Standalone mode is suitable only for technical validation, not commercial operation.

2. **Closed-loop ring-elevator economics depend heavily on life and throughput**:
   - Conservative (100-year life): 1.73 million CNY/ton, still about **100x** above reusable rockets (~17,000 CNY/ton).
   - Optimistic (500-year life, annual throughput 3.4 in 10k tons): 340,000 CNY/ton, still around **20x** above reusable rockets.
   - Ring-elevator cost advantage appears only under **very long amortization periods** and **full-load operation** assumptions.

3. **Fully reusable rockets remain the economically optimal solution in the current and foreseeable future**:
   - Even under conservative assumptions, unit cost is ~17,000 CNY/ton, around **20x cheaper** than optimistic ring-elevator estimates.
   - Rockets do not require century-scale life commitments, iterate faster, and recover capital sooner.

4. **The true value of a ring elevator is not pure cost, but transport capability**:
   - Single-lift capacity around 81 tons with continuous operation, suitable for **very large-mass cargo** (space power station modules, asteroid-mining equipment) in long-term, stable, lower-frequency transport.
   - Reusable rockets are better for **high-frequency, small/medium-batch, time-sensitive** transport.

## VI. Final Takeaway

| Question | Answer |
|:---|:---|
| Is a standalone space elevator worth investing in? | **No**. Capex is too high and throughput too low; unit cost is over 1000x reusable rockets. |
| Can a closed-loop ring elevator become cheaper than reusable rockets? | **No in this model**. Even with 500-year life and full utilization, unit cost is still about 20-100x higher. |
| Does the ring elevator still have value? | **Yes: ultra-large payloads, ultra-long-term, stable lift capacity**. Some non-segmentable giant structures (kilometer-class trusses, ultra-large antennas) may only be practical via ring-elevator systems. |
| What are reusable rockets still weak at? | **Single-item size limits** (fairing and geometry limits; cargo beyond certain dimensions cannot be launched conventionally). |

**One-sentence summary**: In economic terms, ring elevators are weaker than reusable rockets, but in **engineering capability** (size envelope and continuous heavy lift), they can be irreplaceable. Reusable rockets are express delivery; ring elevators are heavy freight rail. They are complementary, not strict substitutes.

## Space Tourism

At present, Chinese commercial space tourism companies (for example, Traverser and CAS Space) sell tickets to healthy people aged 18-75. Ordinary people can participate after basic medical checks and short training. There are already all-civilian mission precedents, such as SpaceX Inspiration4, where all four crew members were non-professional astronauts. Research indicates that ordinary participants without long-term astronaut selection and training differ significantly from professionals in physiological and psychological adaptation; this is both a risk area and a research gap.

### Ordinary People vs Professional Astronauts: Core Differences

| Comparison Dimension | Professional Astronauts | Space Tourists (Ordinary People) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Selection Standard** | Extremely strict, requiring top physical, psychological, and medical criteria. | Basic screening that excludes severe underlying disease (for example, serious heart disease). |
| **Training Duration** | Typically **years** of full-scope training, including spacecraft systems and emergency procedures. | Usually **up to a few months**, focused on safety briefing and basic experience flow. |
| **Mission Nature** | Executes complex tasks such as research and maintenance. | Primary goal is a **safe and comfortable** space experience. |
| **Health Standard** | Long-term health monitoring continues even after retirement. | Recent studies suggest healthy adults can recover after short missions, but available data is still limited. |

### What Does This Kind of "Space Flight" Feel Like?

**1. Physical Sensation: Nothing Like Smooth Airline Flight**

- **High G-load (overload)**: During launch and reentry, passengers may experience about **3-4G**. Compared with about 0.2G sensation in ordinary aircraft takeoff, this can feel like **three to four body weights pressing on you**, with heavy breathing and difficult limb movement.
- **Weightlessness**: Passengers may experience around **3-6 minutes** of microgravity and free-floating. This is fundamentally different from any commercial-airline sensation and may cause spatial disorientation or motion sickness.

**2. Psychological Impact: Potentially Beyond Expectation**

- **Overview effect**: Looking back at Earth from space can trigger profound cognitive shifts, and some people may experience strong emotional fluctuation.
- **Post-landing recovery period**: Recent findings indicate that after severe gravity transitions, **postural stability (balance) may still be incompletely recovered after one week**. After landing, people may still feel unsteady.

**3. Risks and Cost: Expensive, and Not a Casual Experience**

- **Health risks**: Main challenges are tolerance of high G-loads and adaptation to microgravity, both recognized as potential triggers for latent conditions.
- **High ticket prices**: Current domestic suborbital trip prices are around **3 million CNY**, while SpaceX orbital tourism can exceed **USD 50 million**. Price is clearly the major barrier for most people.

### Summary: A Cautious, Premium Adventure

The conclusion is straightforward: **the body sensation of crewed rocket flight is a rigorously engineered, high-intensity, roller-coaster-like extreme experience, and is fundamentally unlike the smooth journey of civil aviation.**

For ordinary people, this is a premium adventure with real physiological challenge, very high cost, and non-trivial risk. It relies on solid engineering, strict medical screening, and careful mission assurance for safety, but in essence and comfort, it remains very different from conventional air travel.
