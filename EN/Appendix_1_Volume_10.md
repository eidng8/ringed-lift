## 10.2 Core strategic positioning: the independent value of the Moon industry

###10.2.1 Redefining: Moon industry is not an accessory to the ladder

The design, investment and construction of the lunar industrial system are not premised on the existence of the ladder, but have an independent strategic logic. Even if never proposed, the establishment of a permanent industrial base in the lunar polar zone is also economically, technically and strategically sound — the low gravity of the Moon (1/6 g), the natural high vacuum (approximately 10-12 bar), the absence of an atmosphere, the persistence of light (an arctic ceremonial sun > 90 per cent), the abundance of water ice and mineral resources, so that they are naturally suitable for being a resupply port for deep exploration, a site for the manufacture of high-value industrial products and the first permanent settlement outside human civilization. Conversely, the technology and investment risks of the ring ladder would increase significantly due to the lack of redundant supply sources, even though it would be possible to do so under the Earth's launch baseline programme without the involvement of the Moon (although it would be more costly and there would be no strategic redundancy on the supply chain).

Therefore, the relationship between the two projects should not be understood as “the Moon serves the TEST” but as an equal strategic partnership: ** The lunar industry is the first largest customer, not a prerequisite for its survival; the lunar industry is the most important source of quality supply and strategic redundancy for the lunar industry, rather than an optional “accelerator option”. **

The Business Moon Economic Site Guide (LunA-10 Project), published by DARPA in 2025, clearly states that it is technically feasible to establish a self-sustaining and profitable Moon economy on the surface of the Moon by 2035. This 230-page blueprint envisages a complete commercial closed loop from transport, communications to ISRU, and its justification is independent of the construction of any large Earth orbit facility [1]. This confirms the independent value of the Moon economy at the strategic planning level.

Thus, there is no need for a strong alignment of the rhythms of the two projects on the time lines of financing, construction and operation. The core clients of the lunar industry are always the entire lunar space economy — the most important and largest of them — but are by no means the only ones and do not constitute a necessary condition for the lunar industry to survive.

Forward technology facilities: non-quantifiable civilizational values

It is almost impossible to accurately quantify the economic returns of the following facilities in the financial model, as they do not produce cash flows, but rather the expansion of human cosmological boundaries. But in assessing the overall civilizational value of the lunar industrial system, these facilities are the main vehicles of ultimate return.

** (a) Radio Astronomy Array on the Back of the Moon**

The back of the moon is the quietest area known to mankind in the solar system. All man-made radio noise from the Earth is completely shielded from the entire Moon body (3,474 km in diameter), a condition that cannot be observed by any radio telescope in Earth orbit. In this silent world, astronomers are expected to capture, for the first time, the very weak hydrogen signal (1.42 GHz) from the “dark age” of the universe (about 400,000 to 1 billion years after the Big Bang).

Several practical implementation programmes are already under way: NASA ' s FARSIDE project plans to deploy 128 antenna nodes on the back of the Moon, spread over the 12x12 km region, covering 100 kHz to 40 MHz bands [20]. ESA ' s ALO (Astronomical Moon Observatory) concept has entered the pre-study phase of the Argonaut Lander Scheme [21]. The more prospective FarView programme envisages the deployment of approximately 100,000 base station antennas covering approximately 120 km2 of super-largely rare arrays.

** (b) Lunar Gravity Wave Observatory**

Gravity wave astronomy is facing a physical blank frequency range - the “decihertz frequency range” between ground-based detectors (LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA, about 10 Hz-10 kHz) and space detectors (LISA, about 0.1 mHz-0.1 Hz). The Moon provides the ideal platform to fill this gap: The moon's seismic background is extremely low due to lack of plate construction, ocean waves and atmospheric storms.

Preliminary studies of the Lunar Gravity Wave antenna (LGWA) show that the high sensitivity inertial sensor arrays deployed on the surface of the Moon can cover a frequency range of approximately 1 mHz to 1 Hz and can observe gravitational wave signals from a double-white dwarf, a medium-mass black hole and a pre-maturity black hole at the core of distant galaxies [22]. A lunar surface network of 4-6 superconductive accelerometers is sufficient to construct a gravitational wave observatory that is far more sensitive than the existing Earth detectors.

** (d) Moon Deep Space Detection outpost**

The Moon Industrial Base is a natural ideal springboard for all deep-space missions.
